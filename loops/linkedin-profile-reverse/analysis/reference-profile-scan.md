### Headline Anatomy
- Leads with CURRENT ROLE (Editor of Latent.Space) — not job title category
- Follows with MOVEMENT he's fostering (Rise of the AI Engineer) — gives him intellectual ownership of a concept
- Doesn't list tech stacks — lists concepts/ideas/communities instead
- Tone: mission-driven, not job-seeking

### About Section Patterns
- Origin story: quantum finance → coding → developer advocate
- Signature concept: "Learn in Public" — he coined a movement, not just practiced it
- Key career claim: three devtools unicorns (Netlify, Temporal, Airbyte) — specificity with impressive context
- Community scale: "React subreddit for over 200,000 developers" — numbers on community work
- No buzzwords. No "passionate about" language.
- His about section is effectively a narrative of intellectual influence: what he coined, what he built, what he runs

### What Makes This Compelling
- He owns a concept ("Learn in Public," "Rise of the AI Engineer") rather than just a skill set
- Career trajectory shows credibility: finance → code → devrel at scale → founder
- Numbers appear where they matter (unicorn count, community size) but don't dominate
- The profile makes him sound like a *character* in the industry, not a candidate

### What Builders Can Learn From This
- Give yourself a concept, not just a job title
- Name the companies (unicorns if applicable) not just the role category
- Frame community work with scale numbers

## Profile 5: Generic Developer Profile — What Good Looks Like

From multiple guide sources and verbatim examples:

### Strong Headline Formula
`[Role] | [Top 2 Tech Stacks] | [Impact/Results or Career Focus]`

Real examples rated "good" by hiring platforms:
- "Full-Stack Developer | Bridging the Gap Between Engineering & Product | Node.js, React, TypeScript, PostgreSQL"
- "Backend Engineer | Python, AWS | Scaling APIs for fast-growing startups"
- "Full-Stack Developer | MERN | Turning ideas into production-ready apps"
- "Security Engineer | Building and scaling secure payment systems | Ex-FinTech | AWS Certified"
- "AI/ML Product Manager | Shipping Generative AI Products That Solve Real-World Business Problems | Python, SQL"

What works:
- Action verbs ("Scaling," "Turning ideas into," "Shipping," "Building")
- Named tech stack as searchable keywords
- Some signal of orientation beyond pure job-seeking (mission, niche, product focus)

What doesn't work:
- "Software Engineer" (too generic, no differentiation)
- "Passionate Developer | Innovative | Strategic | Expert | Creative" (buzzword soup)
- "Code Ninja" / "Rockstar Engineer" (unprofessional, unsearchable)

### About Section Structure
The consensus strong structure (from multiple sources):

```
[Hook — 1-2 lines, shown before "see more"]
[Origin story or strong framing sentence]
[What you do now / your superpower]
[Specific achievements with numbers]
[Side projects or notable builds]
[CTA — link to portfolio, GitHub, email]
```

Example opening lines from well-reviewed profiles:
- "I caught fire coding." (Katrina, frontend developer) — emotional, specific, memorable
- "Isn't it crazy what zeros and ones can do?" — curiosity hook
- "It all began with a Raspberry Pi." — origin story hook
- "My mother once called me a 'digital gardener.'" — personality, metaphor

Full verbatim example (mid-level engineer, rated strong):
> "I'm a software engineer passionate about building fast, scalable applications. Over the past 3 years, I've worked on backend services in Python and AWS, improving API response times by up to 40%. Currently exploring opportunities in cloud-native development and open-source contributions."

Full verbatim example (senior engineer narrative style):
> "My journey in software engineering began with a fascination for solving puzzles and a Commodore 64. Over the years, my fascination transformed into a career that I'm deeply passionate about. At TechWave Solutions, I led a team of developers to deliver a critical project which increased the company's efficiency by 40%. With over a decade of experience in this field, I've worn many hats - coder, tester, strategist, mentor."

Full verbatim example (full-stack, action-forward):
> "As a frontend developer, I love creating clean, user-friendly interfaces. I've delivered React-based products used by 10,000+ active users and collaborated with cross-functional teams to launch features ahead of schedule. Always curious about design systems and accessibility."

### Experience Entry Format (what works for developers)
The winning structure:
- 2–3 sentence narrative opening (role context, scope)
- 3–6 bullet points, each starting with action verb
- At least 1–2 bullets with quantified impact
- Name the tech stack within the bullets (not just in skills section)

Strong bullet examples:
- "Built and deployed a microservices architecture on AWS that scaled to 50,000 users without downtime."
- "Optimized SQL queries, reducing server costs by 20% while improving performance."
- "Led a 4-member team to deliver a client-facing React dashboard within 3 months."
- "Automated CI/CD pipelines with GitHub Actions, cutting release cycles from 2 weeks to 3 days."
- "Launched AI-powered reporting feature used by 60% of customers within 3 months, driving $2M in upsell revenue."

STAR method applied to bullets: Action verb → Scope/Context → Result with number

### Featured Section — What Builders Pin
Strong Featured choices for developers/builders:
1. Link to primary GitHub profile or flagship repo
2. Link to personal portfolio/website or blog
3. Demo video or live product link
4. A LinkedIn post that performed well (shows engagement)
5. Published writing (article, blog, newsletter)
6. A notable open source project README

What makes Featured work:
- Visual thumbnails beat plain links — use URLs with rich OG previews
- 4–6 items is the sweet spot
- Rotate every few months to signal active development
- For founders: pin the product itself (landing page) as first item

Featured is the section most profiles skip. This is a mistake — it's the first visible media after the About section and serves as a "proof of work" showcase.

### Skills Section
- LinkedIn's algorithm uses Skills for search ranking — this is SEO for your profile
- Endorsements on skills increase credibility signals
- Strong ordering: Lead with your most distinctive/impressive skills, not the most generic
- For developers: Lead with the stack you want to do next, not just what you've done
- Mix technical skills (Python, React, AWS Lambda) with domain skills (API Design, Distributed Systems) and a few meta-skills (Technical Writing, Developer Relations)

## Cross-Profile Patterns: What Separates Compelling From Forgettable

### Pattern 1: Concept Ownership vs. Job Title Ownership
Compelling: "I coined 'Learn in Public'" / "Creator of Datasette" / "Author of MAKE"
Forgettable: "Senior Software Engineer at XYZ Corp"

The people with the best profiles don't describe their role — they describe what they created or changed.

### Pattern 2: Named Products vs. Generic Responsibilities
Compelling: "Built Nomad List and Remote OK ($3M ARR)" / "Led DX at Netlify, Temporal, Airbyte (all unicorns)"
Forgettable: "Led developer relations for a fast-growing startup" / "Built production applications for enterprise clients"

Named things are searchable and verifiable. Generic things are not.

### Pattern 3: Numbers Where They Matter
The best profiles use numbers sparingly but effectively:
- Scale numbers (200,000+ developers, 50,000 users, $2M ARR)
- Impact numbers (API response time improved by 40%, release cycles cut from 2 weeks to 3 days)
- Credibility numbers (25+ years at Google, 3 devtools unicorns, 941 GitHub repos)

They do NOT number-stuff ("5+ years experience in 12 technologies with 98% client satisfaction").

### Pattern 4: First-Person Voice vs. Third-Person Resume Listing
Compelling: "I caught fire coding." / "I've worn many hats — coder, tester, strategist, mentor."
Forgettable: "Results-driven professional with proven track record of delivery."

LinkedIn's About section is not a resume. It's a letter from you to a stranger.

### Pattern 5: Hook That Shows Before "See More"
The first ~2 lines (approximately 200–300 characters) show without expansion. This is the above-the-fold real estate. Every strong profile earns the click here.

Bad hooks: "I am an experienced software engineer with over X years..."
Good hooks: "I caught fire coding." / "It all began with a Raspberry Pi." / "Isn't it crazy what zeros and ones can do?"

The best hook is either (a) a statement that creates curiosity, (b) a specific claim that earns respect immediately, or (c) a personality line that makes the reader want to know this person.

### Pattern 6: Builder Profiles Deprioritize or Abandon LinkedIn
Multiple noted builders (swyx, levelsio) have either abandoned LinkedIn or treat it as a passive inbox only. Their primary presence lives on:
- Personal domain (swyx.io, levels.io)
- Twitter/X
- GitHub
- Newsletter/podcast

This matters for calibration: if the LinkedIn profile is going to exist, it should funnel people to the real-presence channels (personal site, GitHub, newsletter), not try to replace them.

## What Gets Missed on Most Developer Profiles

1. **The featured section is empty** — most engineers never fill it. This is free, high-visibility real estate.
2. **Experience bullets are responsibility lists, not impact statements** — "Developed APIs for the platform" vs. "Built REST API serving 2M daily requests"
3. **The About section is a resume paste** — dates, job titles, vague claims. No hook, no personality, no story.
4. **Skills are in random order** — LinkedIn weights the first ~3-5 skills most heavily in search
5. **No CTA or contact info in About** — people who want to reach out often can't find how
6. **Custom URL is never set** — linkedin.com/in/wfo-wells vs. linkedin.com/in/first-last is a cosmetic detail that matters for sharing
7. **Side projects are invisible** — the Experience section can include non-employment projects; most engineers never use this

---

## Format Constraints (Critical Numbers)
- **Headline**: ~220 characters max
- **About/Summary**: ~2,600 characters max (first ~200-300 chars show before "see more")
- **Experience description**: ~2,000 characters per entry
- **Skills**: up to 50, but top ~15 matter most for search
- **Featured items**: no hard limit, 4–6 recommended
- **Character limits on headline are tight** — every character matters; pipe separators (|) are standard
