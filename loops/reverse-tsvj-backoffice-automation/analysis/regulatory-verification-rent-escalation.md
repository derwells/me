## Claim 1: NHSB 2024-01 Caps — 2.3% for 2025, 1% for 2026, for Residential Units <= P10,000/month in NCR

**Extracted claim:** NHSB Resolution No. 2024-01 sets a 2.3% cap on annual rent increases for 2025 and a 1% cap for 2026, applicable to residential units with monthly rent of P10,000 or less in the National Capital Region.

### Verification Status: CONFIRMED

### Source 1: Philippine News Agency — "Gov't reduces hike in monthly rent for residential units"
**URL:** https://www.pna.gov.ph/articles/1241056

PNA confirms: "The National Human Settlements Board (NHSB) has set a maximum 2.3 percent increase in rental for residential units with a monthly rate of P10,000 or less, down from last year's 4 percent cap." The article states the cap is effective January 1 to December 31, 2025. For 2026: "A new limit of one percent shall apply to units occupied by the same tenants as of 2025, paying P10,000 or less per month."

### Source 2: Manila Tribune — "2025 cap for P10K-below rent hike set at 2.3%"
**URL:** https://tribune.net.ph/2025/01/05/2025-cap-for-p10k-below-rent-hike-set-at-23

Tribune confirms identical figures: 2.3% for 2025, reduction from 4% in 2024. Coverage: residential units occupied by the same tenants as of 2024 paying P10,000 or less per month.

### Source 3: DHSUD Official Website — NHSB Resolution 2024-01 (PDF)
**URL:** https://dhsud.gov.ph/wp-content/uploads/Laws_Issuances/07_NHSB/NHSB%20Resolution%202024-01%20(Rent%20Control%202025-2026).pdf

The official NHSB resolution hosted on DHSUD's website. The DHSUD NHSB Policies page lists it as "Rent Control Covering the Period January 1, 2025 to December 31, 2026."

### Source 4: Respicio & Co. — "Rent Increase Limits Philippines 2025"
**URL:** https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/rent-increase-limits-philippines-2025

Respicio confirms 2.3% for 2025, 1% for 2026, and notes the caps were "inflation-adjusted" by the 2024 joint resolution. Notes that both S.B. 2278 / H.B. 9805 were filed to replace the short-term resolution cycle with a tiered inflation-indexed schedule.

### Notes
The claim is fully confirmed. The figures are 2.3% (2025) and 1% (2026), applicable to residential units at or below P10,000/month rent, occupied by the **same continuing tenant**. This applies in NCR and highly urbanized cities. Outside NCR, the threshold is P5,000/month.

## Claim 2: Escalation Trigger — Lease Anniversary vs Calendar Year

**Extracted claim:** Does the NHSB rent increase apply at lease anniversary or calendar year? Is it per annum or per lease year?

### Verification Status: CONFIRMED WITH NUANCE — There is a two-layer system

### Source 1: Respicio & Co. — "Legal Limits on Rent Increase Philippines"
**URL:** https://www.lawyer-philippines.com/articles/legal-limits-on-rent-increase-philippines

Respicio states: "Once every 12 months" with the cycle measured from "the last increase or lease execution, not from the calendar year." This establishes the **frequency rule** under RA 9653: the landlord cannot increase rent more than once in a 12-month cycle counted from the lease anniversary or last increase.

### Source 2: Respicio & Co. — "Applicability and Rules of the Rent Control Act (RA 9653) in 2025"
**URL:** https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/applicability-and-rules-of-the-rent-control-act-ra-9653-in-2025

Respicio confirms: "Rent may not be increased more than once a year, calculated from the date of the last increase or the start of the lease." This is the per-lease-year frequency rule.

### Source 3: NHSB Resolution No. 2024-01 (via multiple press reports)
**URL:** https://www.pna.gov.ph/articles/1241056

The NHSB resolution itself operates on a **calendar year** basis: "effective January 1, 2025 to December 31, 2025" for the 2.3% cap, and "effective January 1, 2026 to December 31, 2026" for the 1% cap. The resolution defines the **maximum allowable percentage** per calendar year.

### Source 4: Lamudi — "What You Need to Know About Rent Control Law in the Philippines"
**URL:** https://www.lamudi.com.ph/journal/what-you-need-to-know-about-the-rent-control-law/

Lamudi summarizes the HUDCC/DHSUD guidance: "The new rates issued by HUDCC allow landlords to increase rents anytime within the year as long as it does not exceed the annual percentage cap."

### Notes — CRITICAL NUANCE
There are **two distinct rules** operating simultaneously, and conflating them is a common error:

1. **The cap rate** is set per **calendar year** by the NHSB resolution. For 2025, it is 2.3%. For 2026, it is 1%. The resolution says "effective January 1 to December 31" for each year.

2. **The frequency rule** under RA 9653 is per **lease cycle** — no more than once every 12 months, counted from the date of the last increase or the start of the lease.

**Practical effect:** A landlord whose tenant's lease anniversary falls in, say, July 2025, may increase rent by up to 2.3% at that July anniversary. The landlord cannot increase again until July 2026, at which point the applicable cap is 1%. The calendar-year framing of the resolution sets **which cap applies at the time the increase is imposed**, while the frequency is governed by the lease cycle. A landlord could technically impose the increase at any point during the calendar year (per HUDCC guidance), but only once per 12-month lease cycle.

This means the claim that NHSB increases apply "at lease anniversary" is **partially correct** (the frequency is tied to the lease), while the cap percentage is defined by calendar year. The two rules interlock.

## Claim 3: Compound vs Simple Increase — Original Base Rent or Current Rent?

**Extracted claim:** Does the NHSB-regulated increase apply to the ORIGINAL base rent or to the CURRENT rent (i.e., last year's rate)?

### Verification Status: CONFIRMED — Increase applies to CURRENT rent (compounding effect)

### Source 1: Respicio & Co. — "Applicability and Rules of the Rent Control Act (RA 9653) in 2025"
**URL:** https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/applicability-and-rules-of-the-rent-control-act-ra-9653-in-2025

Respicio explicitly states: "the allowable annual increase is limited to seven percent (7%) of the **current monthly rent**." This means the percentage is applied to whatever the tenant is currently paying.

### Source 2: Respicio & Co. — "Legal Limits on Rent Increase Philippines"
**URL:** https://www.lawyer-philippines.com/articles/legal-limits-on-rent-increase-philippines

This article uses more precise language: "the 7% is applied to the **current** rent, not to cumulative notional inflation." And separately: "the increase is computed on the 'last lawful monthly rent' at each anniversary, not the original base."

### Source 3: Respicio & Co. — "Rent Increase Limits Philippines 2025"
**URL:** https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/rent-increase-limits-philippines-2025

Provides a worked example: "if rent was P10,000 and increases 5% annually, Year 2 is calculated on P10,500, not the original P10,000." This confirms the compounding effect.

### Source 4: Salenga Law Firm — "Understanding the Philippine Rent Control Law (RA 9653)"
**URL:** https://philippinelawfirm.com/other/understanding-the-philippine-rent-control-law-ra-9653/

States: "If your rent went from P4,000 to P5,000 in 2023, a P1,000 increase represents a 25% jump, far exceeding the 7% limit." The comparison is to the current rent, not the original.

### Notes — SEMANTIC CONFLICT IN SOURCES (RESOLVED)
One Respicio article (Legal Limits) describes the mechanism as "No compounding: the 7% is applied to the current rent, not to cumulative notional inflation." This is **semantically confusing** but technically consistent. What the author means is:

- The increase each year is a **simple percentage of the current rent** (not a compound-interest formula on the original base).
- However, because the percentage is applied to the **current** (already-increased) rent each year, the **effect is compounding** over multiple years.

This is the same mathematical result as compound interest, but the author is contrasting it with a hypothetical "inflation-cumulative" formula. The practical answer is clear: **the 2.3% in 2025 applies to the current rent, not the original base rent**. Example: if rent is P9,900 in 2024, the 2025 increase is P9,900 x 2.3% = P227.70, making the new rent P10,127.70.

## Claim 4: Threshold Crossing — What Happens When a Controlled Unit Exceeds P10,000?

**Extracted claim:** If a controlled unit at P9,900/month gets a 2.3% increase (to P10,127.70), does it exit rent control? What happens?

### Verification Status: CONFIRMED — Unit exits coverage upon crossing the threshold

### Source 1: Respicio & Co. — "Legal Limits on Rent Increase Philippines"
**URL:** https://www.lawyer-philippines.com/articles/legal-limits-on-rent-increase-philippines

Respicio states: "Coverage is tenant-based rather than unit-based: if rent later rises above the threshold because of lawful increases, the unit exits coverage when it crosses the cap."

### Source 2: Respicio & Co. — "Residential Unit Exemptions under the Rent Control Act"
**URL:** https://www.lawyer-philippines.com/articles/residential-unit-exemptions-under-the-rent-control-act

Confirms: "Housing units that charge rental rates beyond Ten Thousand Pesos (P10,000) per month are NOT covered by the Rent Control Act." This is a bright-line rule — once the rent exceeds P10,000, the statutory protections no longer apply, regardless of how the unit got there.

### Source 3: JLP Law — "Are You Covered by the Rent Control Law?"
**URL:** https://jlp-law.com/blog/are-you-covered-by-the-rent-control-law/

Confirms the coverage test is based on the monthly rent amount. Units exceeding the threshold are exempt.

### Notes — IMPORTANT PRACTICAL IMPLICATION
This creates a paradox in the P9,900 scenario:

1. A unit at P9,900/month is covered by NHSB 2024-01 (rent <= P10,000).
2. The maximum 2.3% increase yields P9,900 x 1.023 = P10,127.70.
3. At P10,127.70, the unit **exits rent control coverage** because it now exceeds P10,000.
4. For 2026, the unit would **not** be subject to the 1% cap (since it is no longer at or below P10,000).
5. The landlord could theoretically negotiate a higher increase for 2026 under Civil Code rules.

However, the 2026 cap language states it applies to "units occupied by the same tenants as of 2025, paying P10,000 or less per month." If the tenant's rent is P10,127.70 in 2025, the tenant no longer qualifies for the 2026 cap. The unit effectively "graduates" out of rent control through lawful increases.

**This is a real scenario for Las Pinas units near the threshold.** Automation should flag units approaching the P10,000 ceiling and alert the lessor that a lawful increase may push the unit out of controlled status.

## Claim 5: Commercial Lease Escalation — Purely Contractual?

**Extracted claim:** Commercial leases are purely contractual (Civil Code Art. 1306 freedom of contract). No statutory cap exists. No CPI-linked statutory requirement.

### Verification Status: CONFIRMED

### Source 1: Respicio & Co. — "Annual Rent Escalation in Commercial Lease Agreements in the Philippines"
**URL:** https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/annual-rent-escalation-in-commercial-lease-agreements-in-the-philippines

Respicio confirms: "There is no specific statute mandating or prohibiting rent escalation in commercial leases; it is purely a matter of agreement." The article references Article 1305 of the Civil Code (freedom of contract) and Article 1308 (mutuality of contracts). Escalation rates typically range from 5-15% annually, depending on location, property type, and lease duration.

### Source 2: RichestPH — "Understanding Rent Escalation Clauses in Philippine Commercial Leases"
**URL:** https://richestph.com/understanding-rent-escalation-clauses-in-philippine-commercial-leases/

Confirms: "the Philippines doesn't have a specific law that explicitly regulates rent escalation clauses in commercial leases." CPI-linked escalation is described as one voluntary option among several (fixed percentage, CPI-linked, market-based, graduated step-up, hybrid).

### Source 3: Respicio & Co. — "Legal Guidelines on Commercial Property Lease Rate Increases"
**URL:** https://www.lawyer-philippines.com/articles/legal-guidelines-on-commercial-property-lease-rate-increases-in-the-philippines

Confirms commercial leases enjoy "greater freedom of contract" under Article 1305 of the Civil Code. Notes that courts may intervene only if clauses are unconscionable, oppressive, or violate public policy. The "mutuality of contracts" principle (Article 1308) ensures escalation clauses must be fair and consensual.

### Notes
The claim is fully confirmed. **Correction on the article reference:** The relevant Civil Code provision is **Article 1305** (autonomy of contracts / freedom to stipulate terms), not Article 1306 (which deals with the scope of obligatory force of contracts). The substance is the same — both affirm freedom of contract — but the precise citation should be Art. 1305 for "parties may establish such stipulations, clauses, terms and conditions as they may deem convenient, provided they are not contrary to law, morals, good customs, public order, or public policy."

For the Las Pinas rental corporation, this means commercial lease escalation clauses can be set to any rate agreed upon by the parties. Common practice is 5-10% fixed annual increase, or CPI + a fixed margin.

## Claim 6: CPI-Linked Escalation — Any Statutory Mandate?

**Extracted claim:** Is there any Philippine statute that mandates CPI-linked rent increases for any type of lease? Or is CPI-linkage purely contractual?

### Verification Status: CONFIRMED — No statutory mandate for CPI-linked rent increases

### Source 1: Respicio & Co. — "Rent Increase Regulations Under Philippine Law"
**URL:** https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/rent-increase-regulations-under-philippine-law

Respicio explains that CPI/inflation is a **factor the DHSUD/NHSB considers** when setting the allowable caps for controlled units, but there is no automatic CPI-indexing clause in RA 9653 or any implementing regulation. The NHSB sets a fixed percentage per resolution period, informed by (but not mechanically linked to) CPI data.

### Source 2: RichestPH — "Understanding Rent Escalation Clauses in Philippine Commercial Leases"
**URL:** https://richestph.com/understanding-rent-escalation-clauses-in-philippine-commercial-leases/

Confirms CPI-linked escalation is presented as one voluntary contractual option: "Cost of Living Adjustment (COLA) clauses tie rent increases to inflation indices like the Consumer Price Index." This is market practice, not a legal requirement.

### Source 3: LawPhil.net — RA 9653 Full Text
**URL:** https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2009/ra_9653_2009.html

The statute text (Section 6) authorizes HUDCC/DHSUD to "adjust the allowable limit on rental increases per annum; taking into consideration, among others, the National Statistics Office census on rental units, prevailing rental rates, the monthly inflation rate on rentals of the immediately preceding year, and rental price index." CPI is an **input** to the regulator's decision, not an automatic escalation formula.

### Notes
The claim is confirmed. There is **no Philippine statute** that mandates CPI-linked rent increases for any lease type. The distinction is:

- **Controlled residential units (<=P10,000/month):** NHSB sets a fixed cap (currently 2.3%/1%), informed by CPI data but not mechanically linked to it.
- **Uncontrolled residential units (>P10,000/month):** Purely contractual. Parties can agree on CPI-linkage or any other formula.
- **Commercial leases:** Purely contractual. CPI-linkage is common but optional.

For the back-office automation, this means **no automatic CPI-lookup logic is required by law**. If the lease contract specifies a CPI-linked clause, the system needs to implement it per contract terms, not per statutory mandate.

## Claim 7: New Tenant vs Continuing Tenant — Can Lessor Set Any Rate?

**Extracted claim:** NHSB 2024-01 allows the lessor to set any rate for a NEW tenant (vacant unit, first-time occupant).

### Verification Status: CONFIRMED — Vacancy decontrol applies

### Source 1: Philippine News Agency — "Gov't reduces hike in monthly rent for residential units"
**URL:** https://www.pna.gov.ph/articles/1241056

PNA reports: "If the unit becomes vacant in 2025, the lessor may increase the rent of a new tenant beyond the set limit. This increase is permitted because the new tenant is not covered by the aforementioned resolution."

### Source 2: LawPhil.net — RA 9653 Section 4
**URL:** https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2009/ra_9653_2009.html

The statute provides: "when the residential unit becomes vacant, the lessor may set the initial rent for the next lessee." This is the statutory basis for vacancy decontrol, established in the law itself (not just in NHSB resolutions).

### Source 3: PIDS Discussion Paper — "Rent Control in the Philippines: An Update"
**URL:** https://pidswebs.pids.gov.ph/CDN/PUBLICATIONS/pidsdps1640.pdf

The Philippine Institute for Development Studies confirms that RA 9341 introduced vacancy decontrol and that RA 9653 continued the principle: "allowed lessors to set up the initial rent for the next lessees of residential units that have become vacant."

### Source 4: Baguio City Guide — "Gov't Lowers Rent Increase Cap for 2025"
**URL:** https://baguiocityguide.com/govt-lowers-rent-increase-cap-for-2025-what-tenants-and-landlords-need-to-know/

Confirms: "New residential units built and/or leased out in 2025 may set their own rent." This extends beyond vacancy decontrol to also cover newly built units.

### Notes
The claim is confirmed. Vacancy decontrol allows the lessor to set any initial rent for a new tenant. **However**, once the new tenant is in place, subsequent annual increases are again subject to the applicable NHSB cap (if the unit rent is at or below P10,000/month). The decontrol is a one-time reset at tenant turnover, not a permanent exemption.

**Important exception:** For boarding houses, dormitories, rooms, and bed spaces, the "one adjustment per year" rule applies **even for new tenants** (see Claim 8 below). This means a boarding house cannot re-price at vacancy decontrol rates if it has already made its one adjustment for the year.

## Claim 8: Boarding Houses/Dormitories — Only One Adjustment Per Year

**Extracted claim:** NHSB 2024-01 says only "one" adjustment per year for boarding houses and dormitories.

### Verification Status: CONFIRMED

### Source 1: RA 9653 Section 4 (via Supreme Court E-Library)
**URL:** https://elibrary.judiciary.gov.ph/thebookshelf/showdocs/2/25956

The statute itself provides: "in the case of boarding houses, dormitories, rooms and bedspaces offered for rent to students, no increase in rental more than once per year shall be allowed." This is the statutory foundation, not just an NHSB resolution provision.

### Source 2: Philippine News Agency — "Gov't reduces hike in monthly rent for residential units"
**URL:** https://www.pna.gov.ph/articles/1241056

PNA confirms: "In the case of boarding houses, dormitories, rooms, and bedspaces, only one rent adjustment is allowed within 2025, even if the increase limit has not been reached."

### Source 3: GMA News — "Rent increases capped at 2.3% in 2025"
**URL:** https://www.gmanetwork.com/news/money/personalfinance/931995/rent-increases-capped-at-2-3-in-2025/story/

GMA News reports the same provision from the NHSB resolution: boarding houses, dormitories, rooms, and bed spaces may only implement one rent adjustment within the year.

### Source 4: Manila Standard — "Gov't sets rent hike cap at 2.3% for under P10k/month residences"
**URL:** https://manilastandard.net/news/top-stories/314543693/govt-sets-rent-hike-cap-at-2-3-for-under-p10k-month-residences.html

Manila Standard confirms the same rule.

### Notes
The claim is confirmed. The "one adjustment per year" rule for boarding houses/dormitories has **two important implications**:

1. **Even if the tenant changes mid-year**, the boarding house unit can only have one rent adjustment in that calendar year. This differs from standard residential units, where vacancy decontrol allows resetting the rent for a new tenant. For boarding houses, the once-per-year rule overrides vacancy decontrol within the same year.

2. **"Even if the increased limit has not been reached"** — this means a boarding house operator cannot, for example, increase by 1% in March and then increase by another 1.3% in September (even though the total would be within the 2.3% cap). Only one adjustment is permitted.

3. **The statutory text (RA 9653) specifically says "offered for rent to students."** The NHSB resolution broadens this to all boarding houses, dormitories, rooms, and bed spaces — not just those for students. The NHSB resolution may be more expansive than the original statutory text, but this has not been legally challenged.

## Action Items for Parent Analysis

1. **Correct the Civil Code citation**: The freedom of contract provision for commercial leases is **Art. 1305**, not Art. 1306.
2. **Implement threshold-crossing alerts**: The automation should flag residential units approaching the P10,000 ceiling (e.g., P9,500+) and warn that a lawful increase may push them out of rent control.
3. **Dual-layer escalation logic**: The billing system must track both (a) the applicable NHSB cap percentage for the current calendar year, and (b) the 12-month lease-cycle frequency rule. These are independent constraints that both must be satisfied.
4. **Boarding house special handling**: If the portfolio includes boarding house or dormitory units, the system must enforce the one-adjustment-per-year rule at the unit level (not tenant level), blocking re-pricing at vacancy if an adjustment has already been made that calendar year.
5. **No CPI auto-lookup needed by law**: CPI-linked logic should only be built if lease contracts specify it. There is no statutory requirement.
6. **Compounding is the correct calculation**: Year-over-year increases should be computed on the current (last lawful) monthly rent, not the original base rent at lease inception.
