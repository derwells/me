## Detailed Verification

### Claim 1: No-Markup Rule

**Claim:** Landlords can only charge tenants the actual per-kWh cost from Meralco. Legal basis: EPIRA (RA 9136) Section 43, ERC Resolution No. 12 Series of 2009, ERC Resolution No. 08 Series of 2022.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED with correction on cited legal bases**

The core principle (no markup on electricity) is thoroughly confirmed. However, one of the three cited legal bases — ERC Resolution No. 08, Series of 2022 — does NOT relate to sub-metering. It concerns the Amended Rules for Setting Transmission Wheeling Rates (RTWR) for NGCP. The correct citation for sub-metering rules is ERC Resolution No. 21, Series of 2010 (Amended Rules on Reselling, Sub-Metering and Billing) or ERC Resolution No. 21, Series of 2013 (amendments to meter testing under Res 12-2009).

Additionally, ERC Resolution No. 12, Series of 2009 is specifically about "Rules and Procedures for the Test and Maintenance of Electric Meters of Distribution Utilities" — it governs meter testing accuracy, not directly the no-markup rule. It is commonly cited in tenant disputes because it gives tenants the right to demand meter testing, but the no-markup principle itself flows from EPIRA Section 43 and the broader ERC pass-through doctrine.

**Source 1 — Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> "Philippine law is crystal-clear: electricity is a public utility, not a landlord's side-business. Any amount billed above the DU tariff — without a duly approved RES licence — violates EPIRA, the Consumer Act, and the ERC's Magna Carta."
> — [Legal Actions Against Landlords Overcharging for Electricity and Sub-metering](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/legal-actions-against-landlords-overcharging-for-electricity-and-sub-metering)

**Source 2 — Respicio & Co. / Lawyer-Philippines (legal commentary):**
> "The kWh rate passed on to tenants must equal (or be lower than) the DU's blended rate for the billing month. Any 'service fee' must be separately agreed, reasonable, and disclosed in writing."
> — [Landlord Electricity Overcharge on Sub-Metered Commercial Space Philippines](https://www.lawyer-philippines.com/articles/landlord-electricity-overcharge-on-sub-metered-commercial-space-philippines)

**Source 3 — Supreme Court E-Library (official ERC text):**
> ERC Resolution No. 21, S. 2013 references the original rules adopted in Resolution No. 12, Series of 2009, which govern meter testing and accuracy requirements.
> — [ERC Resolution No. 21, S. 2013](https://elibrary.judiciary.gov.ph/thebookshelf/showdocs/10/67751)

**Correction required:** Replace "ERC Resolution No. 08 Series of 2022" with "ERC Resolution No. 21 Series of 2010" (the actual sub-metering rules). Consider citing ERC Resolution No. 12 Series of 2009 for meter accuracy rights rather than the no-markup principle per se.

### Claim 2: Blended Rate Billing Method

**Claim:** The correct method for sub-metered electricity is: Total Meralco bill / Total kWh consumed on master meter x Tenant's kWh consumption. This "blended rate" method is the ACCEPTED method for electricity (unlike water where per-tier billing is required).

**Verdict: CONFIRMED with nuance**

The blended rate (total bill / total kWh = effective rate per kWh, applied to each tenant's consumption) is widely confirmed as the standard and lawful method. Multiple legal commentary sources describe this as the "pass-through" or "pro-rata" method. One source uses the term "blended utility rate" explicitly.

However, one legal commentary source (Respicio & Co. / Lawyer-Philippines) recommends against "blended lump-sum billing" and instead recommends itemizing charges identical to the DU invoice — i.e., breaking down generation, transmission, system loss, distribution, universal charge, FIT-All, and VAT as separate line items. This is a best-practice recommendation for transparency rather than a prohibition on the blended rate method. The blended rate produces the same total amount; the recommendation is about presentation clarity, not arithmetic.

**Source 1 — Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> "A rate overcharge occurs when a landlord charges a rate per kilowatt-hour (kWh) higher than the blended utility rate for the specific billing month."
> — [Landlord Overcharging on Submetered Utilities in Philippines](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/landlord-overcharging-on-submetered-utilities-in-philippines)

**Source 2 — Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> "Rule of thumb: The aggregate peso amount on the tenant's re-billing must equal (a) his pro-rata kWh x utility tariff plus (b) any contractual, separately itemized admin fee — nothing more."
> — [Legality of Landlord Marking Up Electricity Charges Beyond Meralco Rates](https://www.respicio.ph/dear-attorney/legality-of-landlord-marking-up-electricity-charges-beyond-meralco-rates)

**Source 3 — Lawyer-Philippines / Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> "Avoid 'blended' lump-sum billing; itemize charges identical to DU invoice."
> — [Landlord Electricity Overcharge on Sub-Metered Commercial Space Philippines](https://www.lawyer-philippines.com/articles/landlord-electricity-overcharge-on-sub-metered-commercial-space-philippines)

**Nuance:** The blended rate formula itself is correct and lawful. The distinction from water (where per-tier billing is required) is also correct — Meralco residential rates do not use tiered pricing the way Maynilad does, so blending is appropriate. Best practice is to present the blended result with itemized DU charge components for transparency, but this is advisory rather than mandatory.

### Claim 3: Administrative Fees

**Claim:** Landlords MAY charge reasonable administrative/service fees for electricity sub-metering IF explicitly agreed in the rental contract. No published ERC cap exists for such fees.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED with nuance**

Multiple independent sources confirm that administrative fees are permissible if: (a) reasonable, (b) explicitly agreed in the lease contract, and (c) separately itemized from the electricity pass-through cost.

However, the claim that "no published ERC cap exists" requires nuance. While no binding, final cap currently exists, there are relevant reference points:

1. One legal commentary references a commercial lease template citing "ERC Resolution 21-2010" allowing "₱1.00/kWh as allowed under ERC Resolution 21-2010." Another source cites a range of "₱1.00-₱1.50/kWh" for metering and billing services.
2. An ERC Draft Resolution on "Behind-the-Meter Micro-Grids" circulated in February 2025 proposes an explicit cap of ₱0.50/kWh as an admin fee for sub-meter re-billing in commercial buildings. This is not yet final/published as a binding resolution.
3. In ERC Case No. 2013-091, the ERC held that a "5% administrative fee" on tenants' power consumption violated the pass-through principle, ordered a refund with 6% interest, and imposed a ₱100,000 administrative fine. This suggests the ERC's tolerance for admin fees is narrow and case-specific.

**Source 1 — Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> "Administrative fees or maintenance costs for the submeter may be charged but should be reasonable, transparent, and explicitly agreed upon in the rental contract."
> — [Landlord Overcharging on Submetered Utilities in Philippines](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/landlord-overcharging-on-submetered-utilities-in-philippines)

**Source 2 — Lawyer-Philippines / Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> "ERC's allowable administrative fee is usually ₱1.00-₱1.50/kWh for metering and billing services, but it must be explicitly stated in the contract and separately identified on the tenant's statement of account."
> — [Legal Limits on Utility Charges by Commercial Lessors Philippines](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/legal-limits-on-utility-charges-by-commercial-lessors-philippines)

**Source 3 — Lawyer-Philippines / Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> "ERC Draft Resolution on 'Behind-the-Meter Micro-Grids' (circulated Feb 2025): proposes explicit cap of ₱0.50/kWh as admin fee for sub-meter re-billing in commercial buildings."
> — [Landlord Electricity Overcharge on Sub-Metered Commercial Space Philippines](https://www.lawyer-philippines.com/articles/landlord-electricity-overcharge-on-sub-metered-commercial-space-philippines)

**Nuance:** The claim is directionally correct — admin fees are permissible with contractual agreement and no *binding* ERC cap has been published. However, the statement should be qualified: (1) there are informal ERC reference ranges (~₱1.00-₱1.50/kWh), (2) a draft cap of ₱0.50/kWh is under consideration, and (3) the ERC has penalized specific admin fee arrangements in adjudicated cases. A flat "no cap exists" statement may overstate the landlord's latitude.

### Claim 4: Common Area Electricity

**Claim:** Must be allocated as a separate line item; cannot be embedded as a per-kWh markup on unit consumption. Options are: (a) absorb as operating expense in rent, or (b) apportion at cost by floor area or equal split.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

Multiple sources confirm that common area electricity consumption cannot be embedded into per-kWh charges billed to individual tenants. It must either be absorbed by the landlord as a building operating expense or separately apportioned using a transparent formula.

**Source 1 — Respicio & Co. / Lawyer-Philippines (legal commentary):**
> "Common-area consumption (lifts, escalators, facade lighting) must be billed to the lessor's own account, not divided among tenants."
> — [Legal Limits on Utility Charges by Commercial Lessors Philippines](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/legal-limits-on-utility-charges-by-commercial-lessors-philippines)

**Source 2 — Lawyer-Philippines / Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> Identified as a common violation: "Averaging un-metered common areas into tenants' kWh — Double-charges tenants and distorts rate." Typical sanction: "Ordered segregation + refund."
> — [Landlord Electricity Overcharge on Sub-Metered Commercial Space Philippines](https://www.lawyer-philippines.com/articles/landlord-electricity-overcharge-on-sub-metered-commercial-space-philippines)

**Source 3 — Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> "Best practice is to annex a Utility Schedule to the lease, showing the formula for apportionment and a right of tenant inspection."
> — [Electric Submetering and Property Boundaries](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/electric-submetering-and-property-boundaries)

**Additional note:** One source (Legal Limits on Utility Charges by Commercial Lessors) states that common area consumption "must be billed to the lessor's own account, not divided among tenants," which slightly narrows option (b) in the claim. However, other sources confirm that apportionment at cost (by floor area or equal split) is acceptable if done transparently and with contractual agreement. The key prohibition is embedding it in the per-kWh rate.

### Claim 5: Lifeline Rate Disqualification

**Claim:** Individual sub-meter tenants consuming <=100 kWh cannot claim lifeline rates because eligibility attaches to the Meralco master meter account, not individual sub-meters. Master meter aggregate typically exceeds 100 kWh.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED with correction**

The core conclusion (sub-metered tenants cannot claim lifeline rates) is correct, but the reasoning needs updating. The claim frames it purely as a consumption-threshold issue (master meter exceeds 100 kWh), but the actual disqualification is more multi-layered:

1. **Customer of record:** Only the person whose name appears on the Meralco bill can apply. Sub-metered tenants are not customers of record with Meralco.
2. **Condominium/subdivision exclusion:** Under RA 11552, customers residing in condominiums or subdivision projects are explicitly excluded from the lifeline rate (except government housing projects like NHA).
3. **Means-tested eligibility:** Even for the customer of record, lifeline rate now requires being either a 4Ps beneficiary or living below the PSA poverty threshold — consumption below 100 kWh alone is no longer sufficient.
4. **Master meter consumption:** The claim's reasoning (aggregate exceeds 100 kWh) is also true as a practical matter, but it is a secondary factor, not the primary legal basis.

**Source 1 — Meralco (official FAQ):**
> "Only the one whose name appears in the Meralco bill should apply for Lifeline rate." Excluded: "Customers residing in condominiums or subdivision projects."
> — [Lifeline Rate FAQs - Meralco](https://company.meralco.com.ph/news-and-advisories/lifeline-rate-faqs)

**Source 2 — Manila Bulletin (news):**
> "Condominium unit owners and residents inside subdivisions can no longer avail of the subsidized rates under Manila Electric Company's (Meralco) Lifeline Rate program," based on RA 11552.
> — [Condo dwellers no longer qualified for power subsidies](https://mb.com.ph/2021/10/11/condo-dwellers-no-longer-qualified-for-power-subsidies)

**Source 3 — Philippine News Agency (government news):**
> "The ERC has set the Lifeline Consumption Threshold at 100 kWh per month." Qualified beneficiaries must be 4Ps beneficiaries or living below poverty threshold.
> — [Households consuming below 100-kWh urged to avail of discounts](https://www.pna.gov.ph/articles/1216245)

**Correction required:** Rewrite the reasoning to cite the correct primary bases: (1) customer-of-record requirement, (2) RA 11552 condominium/subdivision exclusion, and (3) means-testing requirement — rather than framing it purely as a consumption-threshold issue.

### Claim 6: VAT Treatment of Electricity Pass-Throughs

**Claim:** Utility pass-throughs billed by a VAT-registered lessor to tenants are subject to 12% VAT as part of gross receipts. The tenant's EWT (5%) also applies to pass-through amounts since they are "for the lessor's account."

**Verdict: CONFLICTING**

This is the most problematic claim. The evidence is not uniformly supportive, and the correct treatment depends heavily on how the pass-through is structured.

**On VAT (12%):**

The claim that utility pass-throughs are always subject to 12% VAT is **contradicted** by multiple sources. The prevailing legal position is:

- If the lessor merely re-invoices the exact utility cost at cost with no markup, and properly segregates the billing from rent, the pass-through is **NOT** part of the lessor's gross receipts for VAT purposes.
- If the utility charges are bundled into rent or marked up, they **DO** become part of VATable gross receipts.
- The EOPT Act (RA 11976) codified the exclusion of "amounts earmarked for payment to third (3rd) party or received as reimbursement for payment on behalf of another which do not redound to the benefit of the seller" from the definition of gross receipts.
- Supreme Court precedent (*CIR v. Tours Specialists, Inc.*, G.R. No. 66416, 1990) supports this exclusion.

**On EWT (5%):**

The 5% EWT on rental income is confirmed. Whether EWT applies to utility pass-throughs is more nuanced:
- If utility charges are billed by the lessor and treated as additional rental income, the BIR may apply the 5% EWT.
- If the tenant pays the utility provider directly, EWT does not apply.
- BIR practice has been inconsistent; RMC No. 72-2004 indicates that utility payments coursed through the lessor may be subject to 2% EWT (not 5%) if documented as pass-through expenses rather than rental income.

**Source 1 — Respicio & Co. / Lawyer-Philippines (legal commentary):**
> "Passing-through utility cost is not VAT-able if the landlord merely 're-invoices' the exact amount (no margin, proper documentation)." If a markup is added, "the entire charge becomes part of the vatable gross rental income of the lessor."
> — [Legal Limits on Utility Charges by Commercial Lessors Philippines](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/legal-limits-on-utility-charges-by-commercial-lessors-philippines)

**Source 2 — BDB Law (tax law firm):**
> Under EOPT, "amounts earmarked for payment to third (3rd) party or received as reimbursement for payment on behalf of another which do not redound to the benefit of the seller" are excluded from the VAT base.
> — [VAT On Reimbursements Under EOPT](https://www.bdblaw.com.ph/index.php/newsroom/articles/tax-law-for-business/1148-vat-on-reimbursements-under-eopt)

**Source 3 — Respicio & Co. (legal commentary):**
> EWT on rent is 5% of gross rental. For utility reimbursements coursed through the lessor, the BIR treatment depends on documentation and lease structure.
> — [Withholding Tax on Rent and BIR Form 1601E Requirements](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/withholding-tax-on-rent-and-bir-form-1601e-requirements-in-the-philippines)

**Recommended revision:** The claim should be revised to distinguish between two scenarios: (a) bundled billing (utility charges included in or added to rent) — VAT and EWT apply, and (b) segregated at-cost pass-through (separately billed reimbursement with documentation) — VAT should not apply, and EWT treatment is uncertain. The blanket statement that pass-throughs are "subject to 12% VAT as part of gross receipts" is an overstatement that contradicts EOPT Act provisions and Supreme Court jurisprudence. The EWT rate for pass-throughs (if applicable at all) may be 2% rather than 5% under RMC 72-2004.

### Claim 7: System Loss Cap

**Claim:** ERC has capped system losses at 8.5% of the bill per ERC Resolution No. 17, Series of 2008 (reduced from 9.5%).

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

This claim is straightforwardly confirmed by multiple authoritative sources.

**Source 1 — Supreme Court E-Library (official ERC text):**
> ERC Resolution No. 17, Series of 2008 adopted a new system loss cap. For private distribution utilities: 8.5% of total kWh purchased and generated. For electric cooperatives: 13%. Effective starting January 2010 billing.
> — [ERC Resolution No. 17, S. 2008](https://elibrary.judiciary.gov.ph/thebookshelf/showdocs/11/44166)

**Source 2 — Philstar (news):**
> "The Energy Regulatory Commission (ERC) has set the system loss cap for Meralco at 8.5 percent starting January [2010]."
> — [ERC sets 8.5% system loss cap for Meralco starting January](https://www.philstar.com/business/2009/11/27/526708/erc-sets-85-system-loss-cap-meralco-starting-january)

**Source 3 — Power Philippines (industry news):**
> "The system loss cap was brought down to 8.5 percent by 2010 pursuant to Resolution 17, which the ERC issued on January 16, 2008."
> — [What is System Loss? Here's a quick research](https://powerphilippines.com/system-loss-heres-quick-research/)

**Minor precision note:** The claim says "8.5% of the bill." More precisely, it is 8.5% of total kWh purchased and generated — it caps the kWh that can be charged as system loss, not 8.5% of the peso amount. The previous cap was 9.5% for private DUs (under RA 7832), confirmed as reduced to 8.5%.

## Cross-Cutting Issues Identified

### ERC Resolution Citation Accuracy
The original claims cite "ERC Resolution No. 08 Series of 2022" as a basis for the no-markup rule. This resolution concerns transmission wheeling rates, not sub-metering. The correct sub-metering resolutions are:
- **ERC Resolution No. 12, Series of 2009** — Meter testing rules (gives tenants meter-accuracy rights)
- **ERC Resolution No. 21, Series of 2010** — Rules on Reselling, Sub-Metering and Billing (the core sub-metering regulation)
- **ERC Resolution No. 21, Series of 2013** — Amendments to meter testing rules

### Upcoming Regulatory Changes
- **ERC Draft Resolution on "Behind-the-Meter Micro-Grids" (Feb 2025):** Proposes ₱0.50/kWh cap on admin fees for sub-meter re-billing in commercial buildings. If finalized, this would directly affect Claim 3.
- **DOE Green Building Code Amendment (expected Q4 2025):** Would require smart sub-meters with remote data access for tenants exceeding 50 kW contracted demand.
- **Joint Resolution No. 1, Series of 2026 (Jan 15, 2026):** New lifeline rate implementation rules, including auto-enrollment for 4Ps beneficiaries.

### VAT Treatment Requires Careful Structuring
The VAT treatment of utility pass-throughs (Claim 6) is the most operationally significant finding. The difference between "taxable gross receipt" and "excluded reimbursement" depends entirely on documentation and billing structure. This directly affects how the billing system should format and separate utility charges from rent on invoices and official receipts.
