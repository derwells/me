*Verified: 2026-02-26. Cross-checked each rule against 2-3+ independent PH legal sources.*

## Rule 1: Deposit Collection Caps

**Status: CONFIRMED (with nuance)**

**Rule as stated:** Residential (RA 9653 Sec. 7): Max 2 months deposit + 1 month advance for units ≤ PHP 10,000/mo in Metro Manila. Commercial: No statutory cap (Civil Code Art. 1306 freedom to contract).

**Findings:**

The "1 + 2 rule" is confirmed across all sources. RA 9653 Section 7 explicitly provides that the lessor cannot demand more than one month advance rent and cannot demand more than two months security deposit.

Coverage nuance: The rule applies not only to Metro Manila but also to "other highly urbanized cities" per RA 9653. The PHP 10,000 threshold applies to NCR; other areas may have lower thresholds (PHP 5,000 was the original level for non-NCR areas). NHSB Resolution No. 2024-01 maintains the PHP 10,000 threshold for 2025-2026 coverage.

Commercial: Confirmed — no statutory cap. Civil Code Art. 1306 governs. Multiple sources note the only backstop is the unconscionability doctrine; courts may reduce deposits found contrary to morals or public policy.

**Correction needed:** The rule as stated is slightly too narrow. RA 9653 coverage is not limited to Metro Manila — it covers "residential units in the National Capital Region and other highly urbanized cities." The PHP 10,000 threshold is the current ceiling as extended by NHSB resolutions.

## Rule 2: Bank-Holding Mandate

**Status: CONFIRMED**

**Rule as stated:** RA 9653 Sec. 7 requires lessor to keep deposit in a bank account during the entire lease duration. All interest earned must be returned to lessee at lease end.

**Findings:**

Multiple independent sources confirm the exact statutory language: the deposit "shall be kept in a bank under the lessor's account name during the entire duration of the lease agreement" and "any and all interest that shall accrue therein shall be returned to the lessee at the expiration of the lease contract."

This is a mandatory obligation specific to rent-controlled residential units. There is no equivalent statutory mandate for commercial leases, though the trust money doctrine (Art. 1456) still applies.

One source (Respicio) notes that the interest "may be applied to future rent or handed over in cash upon request" — though the primary statutory text says returned "at the expiration of the lease contract."

**No correction needed.**

## Rule 3: Return Timeline

**Status: CONFIRMED (with important nuance on the statutory text)**

**Rule as stated:** Residential controlled: Must return within 1 month of lease expiry AND actual turnover (RA 9653 Sec. 7). Commercial: No specific statutory deadline; "reasonable time" standard; 30-day benchmark from jurisprudence. Delay penalty: 6% per annum legal interest from date of formal demand (BSP Circular 799-2013, Nacar v. Gallery Frames).

**Findings:**

**Residential controlled:** Multiple sources confirm a one-month return period. However, there is a nuance: RA 9653 Section 7 itself says the deposit and interest shall be returned "at the expiration of the lease contract." The specific "one month" timeline is referenced in multiple legal commentary sources (Respicio, RichestPH, Salenga Law) as either derived from implementing rules/practice or from the statutory phrase read together with the "reasonable time" standard. The one-month standard is widely accepted and cited, but users should be aware that the statutory text does not contain the literal phrase "within one month" — it is a bright-line consensus interpretation. The phrasing "one month after surrender" appears in multiple independent legal analyses as the operationalized standard.

**Commercial:** Confirmed. The Civil Code has no specific deadline. The 30-day benchmark is a practice/jurisprudential norm, not statutory. Contracts may stipulate 15-60 days.

**Delay penalty:** Confirmed. BSP Circular No. 799, Series of 2013 (effective July 1, 2013) sets the legal interest rate at 6% per annum, replacing the previous 12%. Nacar v. Gallery Frames (G.R. No. 189871, August 13, 2013) is the landmark Supreme Court case harmonizing the 6% rate. The interest runs from the date of formal (judicial or extrajudicial) demand per Art. 2209, not from the date the deposit becomes due.

**No correction needed, but nuance should be noted:** The "1 month" standard for residential is a universally cited interpretation rather than a verbatim statutory number. It is reliable for system design purposes.

## Rule 4: Deduction Rules

**Status: CONFIRMED (with one case citation correction)**

**Rule as stated:** Allowable: unpaid rent, unpaid utilities, damage beyond normal wear and tear. Not allowable: routine repainting, nail holes, normal appliance wear. Requirement: itemized statement with receipts/quotations (Radiowealth Finance v. Palacol). Burden of proof on landlord. "Automatic forfeiture" clauses void under Art. 1306/1409.

**Findings:**

**Allowable/Not allowable deductions:** Fully confirmed across all sources. RA 9653 Sec. 7 itself provides the forfeiture list: "rent, electric, telephone, water or such other utility bills" and destruction of "house components and accessories." Multiple legal commentaries confirm that normal wear and tear (faded paint, minor nail holes, normal appliance deterioration) cannot be charged.

**Itemized statement requirement:** Confirmed as a general jurisprudential principle — landlords must substantiate deductions with dated photos, turnover checklists, receipts, and quotations. The burden of proof rests on the landlord.

**CORRECTION on case citation:** "Radiowealth Finance v. Palacol" does not appear to exist as a Philippine Supreme Court case. The searches returned:
- *Radiowealth Finance Co. v. Del Rosario* (G.R. No. 138739, July 6, 2000) — a promissory note collection case, not a lease/deposit case.
- *Radiowealth Finance Co. v. Palileo* (G.R. No. 83432, May 20, 1991) — a double sale of unregistered land case, not a lease/deposit case.

Neither case establishes the itemized receipt requirement for security deposit deductions. The itemized statement requirement for deposits appears to derive from general Civil Code obligations (Arts. 1170, 1173 on good faith and diligence; Art. 19 on abuse of rights) and from the RA 9653 framework itself (which implies accounting by specifying amounts "commensurate to the pecuniary damage"). The case citation "Radiowealth Finance v. Palacol" should be treated as **UNVERIFIED** and likely erroneous. The underlying legal principle (itemized deductions required, burden on landlord) is confirmed from other sources.

**Automatic forfeiture clauses:** Confirmed. Courts have consistently struck down or moderated blanket forfeiture clauses. Art. 1229 (judicial reduction of unconscionable penalties) is the key mechanism, working together with Arts. 1306 and 1409. *Lee v. Court of Appeals* (G.R. No. 12455, 1999) and *Gonzales v. Tiru* (G.R. No. 131445) are actual confirmed cases on point.

**Correction needed:** Replace "Radiowealth Finance v. Palacol" with the correct legal basis: general Civil Code principles (Arts. 1170, 1173, 19) and RA 9653 Sec. 7's "commensurate to the pecuniary damage" language. The case citation appears to be fabricated or misattributed.

## Rule 5: Tax Treatment at Receipt

**Status: CONFIRMED**

**Rule as stated:** BIR Ruling DA-334-2004: Refundable security deposits NOT income at receipt. RMC 11-2024: Recorded as liability by lessor. Advance rent: taxable at receipt (substance over form test).

**Findings:**

**BIR Ruling DA-334-2004:** Confirmed. Refundable security deposits are not considered income and are not subject to withholding at the time of receipt. The deposit is recorded as a liability on the lessor's books. Consistent with BIR Ruling No. DA-489-03 (same principle).

**RMC 11-2024:** Confirmed. Issued January 22, 2024, it clarifies the tax treatment of lease accounting under PFRS 16. Security deposits are recorded as an asset by the lessee and a liability by the lessor. The security deposit is not immediately treated as a deductible expense (lessee) or taxable income (lessor) at the time of payment/receipt. The deduction/income is only recognized when the security deposit is actually applied to the lease.

**Advance rent vs. deposit distinction:** Confirmed. Advance rent is immediately taxable upon receipt per BIR Ruling No. 118-12. The BIR applies a substance-over-form test: if the money is earmarked as the last month's rent and non-refundable, it is advance rent regardless of what the parties label it. The key question is whether the lessor has an obligation to refund.

**No correction needed.**

## Rule 6: Tax Treatment at Application/Forfeiture

**Status: CONFIRMED (with partial source gap on two specific rulings)**

**Rule as stated:** BIR Ruling 118-12: Taxable upon application/forfeiture. Becomes gross receipts: subject to 12% VAT (if VAT-registered, RR 16-2005, BIR Ruling DA-593-04). Subject to 5% EWT as rental income (RR 2-98 as amended). Income recognition for RCIT purposes (BIR Rulings 025-02, 033-00).

**Findings:**

**BIR Ruling 118-12:** Confirmed. Security deposits become taxable upon application or forfeiture. The "all-events test" under RR No. 2-98 governs: income is accruable when the right to receive it is fixed and the amount is determinable. Multiple independent sources confirm this principle.

**12% VAT on forfeiture:** Confirmed. RR No. 16-2005 specifies that amounts forfeited for lease-related breaches are part of gross receipts for VAT purposes. Multiple sources confirm the 12% VAT applies when the lessor is VAT-registered (gross receipts > PHP 3M/year). Residential leases at or below PHP 15,000/month are permanently VAT-exempt per NIRC Sec. 109(1)(Q).

**BIR Ruling DA-593-04:** Not independently located in search results. The closest confirmed ruling is BIR Ruling DA-489-03, which establishes the same principle (forfeited deposits are taxable income). The DA-593-04 number is cited in the input file but could not be independently verified against a primary source. The legal principle it supposedly establishes (VATable forfeited deposits) is confirmed through RR 16-2005 and other rulings.

**5% EWT:** Confirmed. RR No. 2-98 as amended, Section 2.57.2(B), imposes 5% EWT on gross rental of real property. RMC 11-2024 confirms that security deposits become subject to EWT when applied to the lease. Withholding obligation is on the lessee (if corporate); otherwise, the lessor must self-assess.

**BIR Rulings 025-02 and 033-00:** BIR Ruling 033-00 is confirmed — forfeited rental deposits are income to the lessor and potentially deductible losses to the lessee. BIR Ruling 025-02 was referenced indirectly (forfeited deposits as part of gross receipts) but the specific ruling number could not be independently located via primary sources. The principle is well-established regardless.

**No correction needed to the rules themselves.** The specific ruling numbers DA-593-04 and 025-02 could not be independently verified, but the legal principles they represent are confirmed through other authoritative sources.

## Rule 7: Interest Obligations Differentiation

**Status: CONFIRMED**

**Rule as stated:** Residential controlled: MUST return all bank interest earned on deposit (RA 9653 Sec. 7). Non-covered residential: No statutory requirement, contractual only. Commercial: No statutory requirement; 6% legal rate only on delayed return after demand.

**Findings:**

**Residential controlled:** Confirmed. Statutory text: "any and all interest that shall accrue therein shall be returned to the lessee at the expiration of the lease contract." This is mandatory.

**Non-covered residential:** Confirmed. Without RA 9653 coverage, there is no statutory obligation to pay interest on the deposit. The only basis for interest would be contractual.

**Commercial:** Confirmed. No statutory interest obligation on the deposit during the lease term. The 6% legal rate (BSP Circular 799-2013, Nacar v. Gallery Frames) applies only in the event of delayed return after formal demand, per Art. 2209 of the Civil Code ("If the obligation consists in the payment of a sum of money, and the debtor incurs in delay, the indemnity for damages, there being no stipulation to the contrary, shall be the payment of the interest agreed upon, and in the absence of stipulation, the legal interest").

**No correction needed.**

## Rule 8: PFRS/Accounting Treatment

**Status: CONFIRMED**

**Rule as stated:** At receipt: DR Cash / CR Security Deposit Liability. At application: DR Security Deposit Liability / CR Rental Income. PFRS 15 reclassification applies on forfeiture for SEC reporting. Must disclose in notes to AFS.

**Findings:**

**Journal entries:** Confirmed. At receipt, the deposit is recorded as a liability by the lessor (DR Cash, CR Security Deposit Liability). At application/forfeiture, the liability is reclassified to income (DR Security Deposit Liability, CR Rental Income).

**PFRS 15 reclassification:** Confirmed. Lessors must reclassify the forfeited deposit from liability to income in their financial statements, complying with PFRS 15 (Revenue from Contracts with Customers), which recognizes revenue when control transfers. Multiple sources (Respicio, Grant Thornton, KPMG) confirm this.

**PFRS 16 for the lessee side:** Refundable security deposits are considered financial assets measured at present value under PFRS 16 (on the lessee's books). RMC 11-2024 confirmed that for tax purposes, the PFRS 16 accounting treatment (ROUA depreciation, lease liability interest) is NOT deductible — only actual rent is.

**Notes to AFS:** Confirmed by general SEC reporting requirements for SEC-registered corporations. Security deposit liabilities and any reclassifications must be disclosed.

**No correction needed.**

## Rule 9: Estafa Exposure

**Status: CONFIRMED (with nuance on threshold)**

**Rule as stated:** Misappropriation of deposit with no intent to return may constitute estafa under RPC Art. 315. Trust money character (Art. 1456) supports this theory.

**Findings:**

**Estafa under Art. 315(1)(b):** Confirmed. The elements of estafa by misappropriation are: (a) receipt of money in trust or under obligation to return; (b) misappropriation or conversion; (c) prejudice to another; (d) demand by the offended party. Security deposits are held in a fiduciary capacity — the landlord holds trust money with a duty to return. Using deposits for operational expenses constitutes breach of trust.

**Art. 1456 implied trust:** Confirmed. "If property is acquired through mistake or fraud, the person obtaining it is, by force of law, considered a trustee of an implied trust for the benefit of the person from whom the property comes." The trust money character of security deposits strengthens the estafa theory by establishing that the money was received under a fiduciary obligation.

**Important nuance:** Not every failure to return a deposit constitutes estafa. The key distinction is between criminal misappropriation (using the funds for personal purposes despite the trust obligation, with intent not to return) versus a civil dispute (good-faith disagreement over deductions). Only the former can give rise to criminal charges. Philippine courts are cautious about criminalizing what are essentially civil contract disputes. The demand element (element d) is also critical — estafa under Art. 315(1)(b) requires that a demand for return was made.

**Jurisprudential support:** *Legaspi v. People* (G.R. No. 225753, 2018) confirms the elements framework for estafa by misappropriation.

**No correction needed, but the nuance about the civil/criminal distinction should be noted in any system design.**

## Rule 10: Deposit Application Order

**Status: CONFIRMED (with clarification)**

**Rule as stated:** When tenant has both unpaid rent AND unpaid utilities, how is the deposit applied? Does Art. 1252-1254 (payment allocation) apply to deposits? Does the lessor have discretion or must specific allocation rules be followed?

**Findings:**

**Art. 1252-1254 applicability:** Confirmed as applicable by analogy. The security deposit application scenario maps to the "application of payments" framework when there are multiple obligations owed by the same debtor (tenant) to the same creditor (landlord).

**Hierarchy confirmed:**

1. **Contract controls first** — If the lease specifies an application order for the deposit (e.g., "deposit shall first be applied to unpaid rent, then utilities, then damages"), the contractual stipulation governs.

2. **If contract is silent, Art. 1252 applies:**
   - **The debtor (tenant) has the primary right** to designate which obligation the deposit should satisfy first.
   - **If the tenant does not designate**, the creditor (landlord) may apply the deposit as they see fit (per Art. 1252, second paragraph — if the debtor accepts a receipt specifying application, they cannot complain).
   - Art. 1253 requires interest to be covered before principal.

3. **If neither party designates, Art. 1254's residual rule applies:** The deposit is applied to the **most onerous debt first**. If debts are of the same nature and burden, application is **proportionate**.

**Practical implication:** In most lease scenarios, the contract will specify what the deposit covers (RA 9653 Sec. 7 itself lists "rent, electric, telephone, water or such other utility bills" and property damage). If the contract is silent on priority, the lessor generally has practical discretion (since the tenant has typically already vacated and is not actively "designating" at the time of application), but must account for the allocation transparently. If challenged, Art. 1254 (most onerous first, or proportionate) would apply.

**Important clarification:** Articles 1252-1254 technically govern "application of payments" — i.e., when a debtor makes a payment to a creditor with multiple debts. The deposit scenario is slightly different (it is the creditor applying a held fund, not the debtor making a payment). However, Philippine legal commentators apply these provisions by analogy to deposit application. No Supreme Court case directly addresses the Art. 1252-1254 framework in the specific context of security deposit allocation, but the analogy is well-accepted in legal practice.

**No correction needed, but the analogical nature of the application should be noted.**

## Additional Searches: Recent Changes, DHSUD Orders, Co-Mingling

### Recent 2024-2026 Changes to Deposit Rules or Tax Treatment

**Status: No material changes to deposit rules; rent control extended.**

1. **Congress Joint Resolution (December 29, 2024):** Extended and modified RA 9653 parameters for the 2025-2027 cycle. The deposit rules (1+2 rule, bank-holding mandate, interest obligation) remain unchanged.

2. **NHSB Resolution No. 2024-01:** Covers rent control for January 1, 2025 to December 31, 2026. Rent increase caps: 2.3% for 2025, with the PHP 10,000 threshold maintained. No changes to deposit rules.

3. **Potential threshold adjustment:** The 2024 Joint Resolution references raising coverage ceilings to PHP 12,000 (NCR) / PHP 8,000 (non-NCR) to restore 2010 purchasing power. This is under the broader 2025-2027 framework, but NHSB resolutions operationalize the specific thresholds.

4. **Comprehensive Rental Housing Code bills (S.B. 2278 / H.B. 9805):** Filed in 2024 to replace the cycle of short-term joint resolutions with a tiered, inflation-indexed schedule. If enacted, this could change deposit rules — but it has not been enacted as of February 2026.

5. **DHSUD Digital Rent Registry:** A pilot online registry requiring landlords to declare rent, deposits, and increases is being developed. Full rollout targeted for 2026. This could create new disclosure/registration obligations for deposits.

### Co-Mingling of Deposits with Operating Funds

**Status: CONFIRMED — No specific penalty beyond general fiduciary/criminal law.**

There is no separate statutory penalty specifically for "co-mingling security deposits with operating funds." However, the legal consequences are well-established through multiple legal doctrines:

1. **RA 9653 violation:** For covered units, the deposit MUST be in a bank account. Failure to do so is a violation punishable by PHP 25,000-50,000 fine, 1 month 1 day to 6 months imprisonment, or both.

2. **Estafa (RPC Art. 315):** Using security deposits for operational expenses constitutes breach of trust, potentially leading to estafa charges. This is the primary criminal exposure.

3. **Civil Code breach of fiduciary duty:** Art. 1456 (implied trust) and general obligations law (Arts. 1170, 1173) create civil liability for damages, legal interest, and potential moral/exemplary damages if bad faith is proven.

4. **DHSUD administrative sanctions:** DHSUD can suspend or revoke a lessor's business permit for patterns of abuse involving deposit mishandling.

The absence of a specific "co-mingling penalty" means enforcement relies on the convergence of these general legal provisions rather than a dedicated penalty schedule.

## Additional Edge Cases Discovered

1. **Vacancy decontrol for deposits:** When a controlled unit becomes vacant and is re-leased at a higher rate (above PHP 10,000), the new lease exits rent control. The 1+2 deposit cap no longer applies, and the bank-holding mandate ceases. However, if the new rate is still under the threshold, all RA 9653 deposit rules apply to the new tenant.

2. **Tacit reconduction and deposits:** When a fixed-term lease converts to month-to-month via tacit reconduction (Art. 1670), the original deposit obligation carries forward. There is no basis for the landlord to demand a "top-up" unless contractually stipulated.

3. **PFRS 16 present-value measurement:** On the lessee's books, a refundable security deposit that will be returned at lease end should be measured at present value (discounted) as a financial asset under PFRS 9/16. The difference between nominal and present value creates an adjustment that is unwound as interest income over the lease term. This is an accounting nuance that does not affect tax treatment (RMC 11-2024 confirmed only actual amounts are relevant for tax).

4. **Partial forfeiture:** RA 9653 Sec. 7 provides for forfeiture "in the amount commensurate to the pecuniary damage" — not total forfeiture. The excess must be returned. This reinforces the itemized accounting requirement.

5. **EWT timing on application:** Per RMC 11-2024 and RR 2-98, the 5% EWT becomes due at the moment of application/forfeiture, not at original receipt. If the lessee has already vacated and is not present to withhold, the practical question of who files the 2307 arises. The input file notes this correctly (lessor self-assessment for commercial abandonment).
