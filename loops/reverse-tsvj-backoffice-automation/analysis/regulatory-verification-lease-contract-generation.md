## Detailed Verification

### Rule 1: Writing Requirement — Civil Code Art. 1403(2)(e)

**Rule as stated:** Leases >1 year must be in writing to be enforceable (Statute of Frauds).

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

**Source 1 — LawPhil (Civil Code full text, RA 386):**
Article 1403(2)(e) provides that "an agreement for the leasing for a longer period than one year" is unenforceable unless "the same, or some note or memorandum thereof, be in writing, and subscribed by the party charged, or by his agent." The contract is unenforceable, not void — it can be ratified.

**Source 2 — Respicio.ph (Verbal Lease commentary):**
Confirms that under Art. 1403(2)(e), leases exceeding one year must have a written note or memorandum. Also confirms that if the oral lease has already been partially or fully performed (executed), the Statute of Frauds defense no longer applies.

**Source 3 — Chanrobles.com / Paredes vs. Espino (G.R. No. L-23351):**
Supreme Court confirmed that a "note or memorandum" suffices — the full contract itself need not be in writing. The memorandum must embody the essentials and be signed by the party charged.

**Nuances found:**
- The effect is "unenforceable" (not void). The contract exists but cannot be enforced by court action without the writing.
- A note or memorandum suffices; the entire contract need not be in writing.
- Executed (performed) contracts are exempt from the Statute of Frauds defense.
- None of these nuances contradict the stated rule; the rule is materially correct.

**Sources:**
- [LawPhil — Civil Code RA 386](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra1949/ra_386_1949.html)
- [Respicio.ph — Verbal Lease, Early Termination](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/verbal-lease-early-termination-and-deposit-refunds-in-the-philippines)
- [ChanRobles — Paredes vs. Espino](https://www.chanrobles.com/scdecisions/jurisprudence1968/mar1968/gr_l-23351_1968.php)

### Rule 2: Notarization Requirements

**Rule as stated:** Not strictly required for validity between parties, but needed for: (a) Registry of Deeds registration, (b) DST filing, (c) court admissibility as public document. 2004 Rules on Notarial Practice require in-person signing, valid IDs, at least 3 originals.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED with nuance**

**Source 1 — Respicio.ph (Validity of Unnotarized Lease Contract):**
Confirms unnotarized lease is "perfectly valid between the parties so long as the essential requisites of consent, subject matter, and cause are present." Notarization "elevates the instrument to public status, simplifying its admissibility in court" and "is a pre-condition for registration, which alone makes the lease binding upon third parties."

**Source 2 — Respicio.ph (Comprehensive Guide on Notarization):**
Confirms the 2004 Rules on Notarial Practice (A.M. No. 02-8-13-SC) require: (a) personal appearance of signatories, (b) competent evidence of identity (government-issued ID with photo and signature), (c) proper venue within the notary's territorial jurisdiction, (d) entries in the notarial register.

**Source 3 — Respicio.ph (Notarial Fee for House Lease):**
Confirms the practice of preparing "at least three (3) originals — Lessor, lessee, and notary each keep one." The notary must keep a copy for safekeeping per Rule VI of the 2004 Rules.

**Nuances found:**
- The "3 originals" requirement is established practice arising from the notary's record-keeping obligations, but the 2004 Rules do not contain a specific numbered provision mandating exactly "three originals." The practice derives from the fact that each party needs one and the notary must retain one. This is functionally equivalent but the characterization as a strict legal requirement should be softened to "standard practice arising from notarial record-keeping rules."
- The claim about DST filing requiring notarization is not directly confirmed. DST is payable on the document itself regardless of notarization status. However, BIR may require the notarized document as an attachment to the filing.
- Court admissibility: An unnotarized document is admissible as a private document (not self-authenticating); notarization makes it a public document with a presumption of regularity (Rule 132, Section 19, Rules of Court).

**Sources:**
- [Respicio.ph — Validity of Unnotarized Lease](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/validity-of-unnotarized-lease-contract-philippines)
- [Respicio.ph — Comprehensive Guide on Notarization](https://www.respicio.ph/dear-attorney/a-comprehensive-guide-on-notarization-in-the-philippines)
- [Respicio.ph — Notarial Fee for House Lease](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/notarial-fee-for-house-lease-philippines)

### Rule 3: Board Resolution for Corporate Lessors

**Rule as stated:** Under RA 11232, corporation's Board of Directors must authorize lease execution via board resolution. Secretary's Certificate must be notarized and accompany the lease. Officers with implied authority (President/CEO) may sign without specific resolution, but best practice is explicit authorization for real property leases.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED with nuance**

**Source 1 — LawPhil (RA 11232 full text):**
Section 35 (now renumbered from old S23): "The corporate powers of all corporations formed under this Code shall be exercised, all business conducted and all property of such corporations controlled and held by the board of directors or trustees."
Section 39: A corporation may "by a majority vote of its board of directors or trustees, sell, lease, exchange, mortgage, pledge, or otherwise dispose of its property and assets" — provided it is not "all or substantially all" of the assets (which would require 2/3 stockholder approval).

**Source 2 — Respicio.ph (Comprehensive Guide to Landlord Documents in Commercial Leasing):**
Confirms: "When dealing with a corporate landlord, a Secretary's Certificate or Board Resolution confirming the authority to lease out the property is vital. It should also name the authorized signatory of the contract."

**Source 3 — ChanRobles (RA 11232 full text):**
Confirms the same Section 39 framework. Also confirms the "usual and regular course of business" exception — if leasing is within the corporation's ordinary business, board majority vote suffices without stockholder approval.

**Nuances found:**
- The rule's claim about "implied authority" of the President/CEO is correct as a general principle of corporate law (the President has authority to act in the ordinary course of business), but RA 11232 does not explicitly codify this for lease transactions. It is jurisprudential.
- For leasing "all or substantially all" corporate assets, the threshold is not just a board resolution but also requires 2/3 stockholder approval per Section 39. The stated rule omits this important distinction.
- If the lease is in the "usual and regular course of business" (e.g., the corporation is a real estate lessor by primary purpose), no stockholder approval is needed at all — just board majority.
- Secretary's Certificate should indeed be notarized as practical requirement, though RA 11232 itself does not specifically mandate notarization of the Secretary's Certificate. It is required by counterparties, banks, and the Registry of Deeds as a matter of established practice.

**Sources:**
- [LawPhil — RA 11232](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2019/ra_11232_2019.html)
- [ChanRobles — RA 11232](https://chanrobles.com/mercomlaw/rano11232-02202019revcorp.php)
- [Respicio.ph — Landlord Documents in Commercial Leasing](https://www.respicio.ph/dear-attorney/the-comprehensive-guide-to-landlord-documents-in-commercial-leasing-in-the-philippines)

### Rule 4: DST on Lease Contracts

**Rule as stated:** NIRC Section 194 — PHP 3 for first PHP 2,000 + PHP 1 per PHP 1,000 (or fractional part) excess, applied per year of lease term. Total DST = annual DST x years. Filed via BIR Form 2000 within 5 days after close of month when document was executed.

**Verdict: CONFLICTING — DST rates are outdated (pre-TRAIN)**

**Source 1 — TaxAcctgCenter.ph (Title VII DST / NIRC as amended by RA 10963):**
Section 194 as amended by the TRAIN Law (RA 10963, effective January 1, 2018) provides: **"Six pesos (P6.00) for the first Two thousand pesos (P2,000), or fractional part thereof, and an additional Two pesos (P2.00) for every One thousand pesos (P1,000) or fractional part thereof, in excess of the first Two thousand pesos (P2,000) for each year of the term."**

The TRAIN Law doubled most DST rates. The pre-TRAIN rates were PHP 3 / PHP 1 — the rates cited in the rule. The current rates are **PHP 6 / PHP 2**.

**Source 2 — TaxAcctgCenter.ph (DST on Lease Agreements tag page):**
Confirms the amended rate of P6.00 for the first P2,000 + P2.00 per P1,000 excess, computed per year of the lease term.

**Source 3 — BIR (Form 2000 Guidelines):**
Confirms BIR Form 2000 for monthly DST filing. Filing deadline is "within five (5) days after the close of the month when the taxable document was made, signed, issued, accepted or transferred." BIR Form 2000-OT is for one-time transactions.

**Source 4 — Revenue Regulations No. 19-2025 (RA 12214 / CMEPA):**
RR 19-2025 did NOT amend Section 194. It only amended Sections 174, 176, 179, and 199 (capital markets instruments). Lease DST rates remain as set by the TRAIN Law.

**Corrections required:**
- **PHP 3 should be PHP 6** for the first PHP 2,000
- **PHP 1 should be PHP 2** per PHP 1,000 excess
- The per-year computation method is correct
- The Total DST = annual DST x years formula is correct
- BIR Form 2000 and the 5-day filing deadline are correct

**Sources:**
- [TaxAcctgCenter.ph — Title VII DST NIRC](https://taxacctgcenter.ph/title-vii-documentary-stamp-tax-nirc-philippines/)
- [TaxAcctgCenter.ph — DST on Lease Agreements](https://taxacctgcenter.ph/tag/dst-on-lease-agreements-philippines/)
- [BIR — Form 2000 Guidelines](https://bir-cdn.bir.gov.ph/local/pdf/2000%20Guide%20Jan%202018.pdf)
- [TaxAcctgCenter.ph — RR 19-2025](https://taxacctgcenter.ph/revenue-regulations-no-19-2025-documentary-stamp-tax-dst-rate-adjustments-and-amendments-under-republic-act-no-12214/)

### Rule 5: Mandatory Contract Clauses

**Rule as stated:** Civil Code Art. 1643 requires: party identification, property description, term/duration, rental rate. RA 9653 mandates deposit/advance limits for covered units. Art. 1650 default subletting restriction (requires lessor consent).

**Verdict: CONFIRMED with correction on Art. 1650**

**Source 1 — LawPhil / ChanRobles (Civil Code Art. 1643):**
Art. 1643: "In the lease of things, one of the parties binds himself to give to the other the enjoyment or use of a thing for a price certain, and for a period which may be definite or indefinite." From this, the essential elements derive: (1) consent/party identification, (2) determinate object (property description), (3) price certain (rental rate), (4) period (term/duration). Confirmed.

**Source 2 — Respicio.ph (Lease Contracts in the Philippines):**
Confirms the four essential elements of Art. 1643. Also confirms that Art. 1318 (general contract requirements: consent, object, cause) applies.

**Source 3 — LawPhil (RA 9653):**
Confirms: maximum deposit is 2 months' rent, maximum advance rent is 1 month's rent for covered residential units (the "1+2 rule"). Deposit must be returned within 1 month after lease ends. Coverage is limited to residential units within specified rent ceilings.

**Source 4 — Respicio.ph (Sublease and Assignment of Lease / Art. 1650):**
**CORRECTION NEEDED:** Article 1650 provides that **"When in the contract of lease of things there is no express prohibition, the lessee may sublet the thing leased, in whole or in part, without prejudice to his responsibility for the performance of the contract toward the lessor."** This is the OPPOSITE of what the rule states. The default rule under Art. 1650 is that subletting IS PERMITTED unless expressly prohibited — not that it "requires lessor consent."

However, there is an important exception: Under RA 9653 (Rent Control Act) Section 8(a), for covered residential units, subleasing without the written consent of the lessor IS prohibited. So the "requires lessor consent" framing applies only to rent-controlled residential units, not as a general Civil Code default.

**Corrections required:**
- Art. 1650: The default rule is that subletting is ALLOWED absent express prohibition (not that it requires consent). The rule as stated inverts the Civil Code default.
- The consent requirement applies specifically to covered residential units under RA 9653 Section 8(a).
- For a corporate lessor drafting commercial leases, the contract should include an express prohibition or consent-required clause if subletting restriction is desired — the law will not supply one by default.

**Sources:**
- [LawPhil — Civil Code RA 386](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra1949/ra_386_1949.html)
- [Respicio.ph — Sublease and Assignment of Lease](https://www.respicio.ph/bar/2025/civil-law/special-contracts/contract-of-lease/sublease-and-assignment-of-lease)
- [LawPhil — RA 9653](https://lawphil.net/statutes/repacts/ra2009/ra_9653_2009.html)
- [Respicio.ph — Legal Grounds for Breach Due to Unauthorized Sub-leasing](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/legal-grounds-for-breach-of-lease-contract-due-to-unauthorized-sub-leasing)

### Rule 6: Registration with Registry of Deeds

**Rule as stated:** Art. 709 — leases >1 year should be registered to bind third parties. Unregistered lease binds only the parties.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED with correction on article number**

**Source 1 — Respicio.ph / ChanRobles (Civil Code provisions):**
The registration requirement for leases exceeding one year is confirmed across multiple sources. However, the more directly applicable provision is **Article 1648** (not Article 709). Art. 1648 provides: "Every lease of real estate may be recorded in the Registry of Property" if the duration exceeds one year. This is the specific lease-registration article.

**Source 2 — Respicio.ph (Real Estate Leasing Laws):**
Confirms: "Leases over one year must be registered (Article 1648); unregistered improvements don't bind third parties." Also: without registration, "the lessee's right to possess the property may be defeated by a new owner."

**Article 709 context:**
Article 709 is in Book II (Property) under the Title on Registry of Property. It establishes the general principle that "titles of ownership or other rights over immovable property which are not duly inscribed or annotated in the Registry of Property shall not prejudice third persons." While Art. 709 does apply broadly to leases as a species of rights over immovable property, **Art. 1648 is the specific lease provision** and is more commonly cited.

**Source 3 — Respicio.ph (Comprehensive Guide to Landlord Documents):**
Confirms the practical effect: "Only a registered, notarized lease annotated on the title binds subsequent purchasers or encumbrancers in good faith." Non-registration does not invalidate the lease but limits its enforceability to the parties only.

**Corrections required:**
- The primary citation should be **Art. 1648** (specific to lease registration), not Art. 709 (general registry of property principle). Both support the same conclusion, but Art. 1648 is the more precise reference.
- Art. 709 is not wrong — it is the general principle under which Art. 1648 operates — but citing Art. 1648 is more standard in lease contexts.
- The substantive rule (register to bind third parties; unregistered lease binds only parties) is correct.

**Sources:**
- [Respicio.ph — Real Estate Leasing Laws](https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/real-estate-leasing-laws-and-tenant-rights-philippines)
- [ChanRobles — Civil Code Book IV](https://chanrobles.com/civilcodeofthephilippinesbook4.htm)
- [ChanRobles — Civil Code Book II](https://chanrobles.com/civilcodeofthephilippinesbook2.htm)
- [Respicio.ph — Landlord Documents in Commercial Leasing](https://www.respicio.ph/dear-attorney/the-comprehensive-guide-to-landlord-documents-in-commercial-leasing-in-the-philippines)

### Rule 7: Lease Document Template Standard Sections

**Rule as stated:** Standard PH lease agreement contains: parties, recitals/WITNESSETH, property description, term, rent, escalation, deposit/advance, utilities, maintenance, termination, governing law, signatures, acknowledgment.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

**Source 1 — Respicio.ph (Lease Contract — Bar Practical Exercises):**
Confirms the standard format: preamble with party identification (name, legal age, citizenship, address, government ID/SEC registration), WITNESSETH recitals, subject of lease (property description with TCT reference), term of lease, rent and deposits, escalation clause, use of premises, obligations of parties (maintenance, repairs, utilities), termination provisions, and acknowledgment (notarial section).

**Source 2 — Respicio.ph (Sample Lease Agreement / Lease Agreement Philippines):**
Confirms the same structural sections. Also includes: assignment/subletting restrictions, improvements/alterations, entire agreement clause, dispute resolution/venue stipulation, and "IN WITNESS WHEREOF" execution block before signatures.

**Source 3 — Respicio.ph (Comprehensive Guide to Landlord Documents):**
Confirms the standard commercial lease format used in the Philippines, including the escalation clause (commonly 5-15% annually or CPI-linked) and the acknowledgment as the notarial closing section.

**Notes:**
- The template list is accurate and comprehensive. Philippine legal practice is consistent in using this structure.
- "Governing law" is sometimes styled as "Venue and Applicable Law" or as part of a general provisions section. This is a minor formatting variation, not a substantive difference.

### Rule 8: DST Exemptions

**Rule as stated:** No exemption for residential vs. commercial or low-rent leases — DST applies to ALL written lease agreements. NIRC Section 199 exemptions are limited to government, diplomatic, agrarian reform.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED with nuance**

**Source 1 — TaxAcctgCenter.ph (Exemptions from DST in Philippines):**
Section 199 enumerates specific exemptions. None relate to residential vs. commercial lease classifications or low-rent thresholds. The exemptions are focused on: (a) fraternal/beneficiary society insurance, (b) government certificates, (c) securities borrowing/lending, (d) installment purchases <P250,000, (e) stock exchange transactions, (f) lease assignments without maturity change, (g)-(n) various financial instruments, and (o)-(p) mutual fund shares (added by RA 12214).

**Source 2 — TaxAcctgCenter.ph (Overview of DST):**
Confirms: "The transactions listed under Section 199 are taxable by nature, but by express provision of the rules, they are exempted from documentary stamp tax." No lease-type or rent-amount exemption exists.

**Source 3 — LawPhil (RA 9243, amending NIRC Section 199):**
RA 9243 expanded DST exemptions primarily for capital market instruments. No lease-specific exemptions were added.

**Nuances found:**
- The characterization of Section 199 exemptions as "limited to government, diplomatic, agrarian reform" is an oversimplification. The actual exemptions are predominantly financial market instruments (stock exchange trades, derivatives, interbranch advances, bank deposits, BSP transactions, etc.), not just government/diplomatic/agrarian.
- Government exemption: Section 199(b) covers certificates required for government services, not a blanket government-entity exemption. Broader government tax exemptions may derive from other provisions (e.g., NIRC Section 27(C) for government-owned and controlled corporations) or special laws, not from Section 199 itself.
- Diplomatic exemptions are not found within Section 199 — they arise from international treaties and the Vienna Convention, implemented through separate BIR rulings.
- Agrarian reform exemptions for DST may arise from RA 6657 (CARP) and RA 11953 (condoning agrarian reform beneficiary loans), not from Section 199.
- The core claim — that there is no residential/commercial distinction or low-rent exemption for lease DST — is **correct**. DST applies to all lease agreements regardless of use type or rent amount.

**Correction needed:**
- The parenthetical characterization of Section 199 exemptions should be revised. Instead of "limited to government, diplomatic, agrarian reform," it should state "primarily financial market instruments; no lease-type, property-use, or rent-amount exemptions exist."

**Sources:**
- [TaxAcctgCenter.ph — Exemptions from DST](https://taxacctgcenter.ph/exemptions-from-documentary-stamp-tax-in-philippines/)
- [TaxAcctgCenter.ph — Title VII DST NIRC](https://taxacctgcenter.ph/title-vii-documentary-stamp-tax-nirc-philippines/)
