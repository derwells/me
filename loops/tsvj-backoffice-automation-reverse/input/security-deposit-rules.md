# Security Deposit Rules — PH Rental Properties

*Fetched: 2026-02-25. Source: lawphil.net, respicio.ph, bir.gov.ph, BDB Law, PwC PH, KPMG PH, Grant Thornton PH.*

---

## 1. Residential (Rent-Controlled) Deposits — RA 9653

**Governing Law:** Republic Act No. 9653 (Rent Control Act of 2009), Section 7

**Coverage:** Residential units in Metro Manila with monthly rent ≤ PHP 10,000, and ≤ PHP 5,000 in other cities. RA 9653 has been repeatedly extended by congressional joint resolution and remains in force as of 2026.

### The "1 + 2 Rule" — Maximum Limits
- **Advance rent:** Maximum one (1) month
- **Security deposit:** Maximum two (2) months' rent

Any contractual stipulation demanding more is void under Civil Code Article 1409 (contracts contrary to public policy are inexistent and void from the beginning).

### Bank-Holding Mandate
RA 9653, Section 7 explicitly requires: the deposit **"shall be kept in a bank under the lessor's account name during the entire duration of the lease agreement."**

This is a mandatory obligation, not optional. The lessor must physically place the funds in a bank account.

### Interest Obligation (Residential)
Under RA 9653, Section 7: **"any and all interest that shall accrue therein shall be returned to the lessee at the expiration of the lease contract."**

This is a statutory obligation, not merely contractual. The lessee is entitled to all bank interest earned on their deposit.

### Penalties for Violation
- Fines up to PHP 100,000
- Imprisonment for repeat offenses
- DHSUD blacklisting

## 2. Commercial Deposits — Civil Code Rules

**Governing Law:** Civil Code of the Philippines (RA 386), Articles 1642–1688 (Title VIII, Lease); Article 1306 (freedom to contract); Article 1456 (implied trusts)

### Deposit Cap
**There is NO statutory cap on security deposits for commercial leases.** The Civil Code does not impose a maximum. Parties are free to agree on any amount, including 3–12 months' gross rent, standby letters of credit, or surety bonds — common in the Philippine commercial property market.

Civil Code Article 1306 provides that parties may not stipulate terms contrary to law, morals, good customs, public order, or public policy. Unconscionable deposit amounts could be struck down, but no bright-line cap exists.

### Implied Trust Doctrine
Civil Code Article 1456 establishes that security deposits function as **trust money** — ownership remains with the lessee. The lessor holds the funds in a fiduciary capacity and cannot treat them as personal income until applied or forfeited.

## 3. Permissible Deductions from Security Deposits

**Legal basis:** Civil Code Articles 1657(1) and 1658 (lessee obligations regarding repairs and damage); RA 9653, Section 7 (forfeiture provision for covered residential units)

### Allowable Deductions
- Unpaid rent
- Unpaid utility bills and association dues
- Damage to the property caused by the tenant **beyond normal wear and tear** (*deterioro extraordinario*)
- Approved repairs/improvements contractually chargeable to the tenant

### Not Allowable
- Routine repainting
- Minor nail holes
- Normal appliance wear
- Any damage attributable to ordinary use

### Documentation Requirement
Per jurisprudence (Radiowealth Finance v. Palacol), an **itemized statement with official receipts or repair quotations** is required. Generic claims are disallowed. The burden of proof rests on the landlord.

### Forfeiture Clause Limits
Clauses declaring the deposit "automatically forfeited" for any breach without notice or accounting have been struck down under Civil Code Articles 1306 and 1409.

## 4. Return Timeline

### Covered Residential Units (RA 9653)
Return must occur within **one (1) month** from both:
(a) expiry/termination of the lease, AND
(b) actual turnover of the premises (keys surrendered, inspection completed)

### Civil Code (Non-Covered Residential and Commercial)
The Civil Code does not prescribe a specific deadline. The standard is "reasonable time" after lease end and turnover. The widely accepted benchmark in practice and jurisprudence is **30 days**, though contracts may specify 15–60 days.

### Penalty for Delay
Under Civil Code Article 1170 and Nacar v. Gallery Frames (G.R. No. 189871, 2013), unreasonable failure to return after proper demand triggers **legal interest at 6% per annum** from the date of formal demand until full payment.

### Criminal Exposure
If a landlord misappropriates the deposit with no intent to return, this may constitute *estafa* under Revised Penal Code, Article 315.

## 6. Tax Treatment of Security Deposits

### 6a. At Receipt — Not Taxable

**BIR Ruling No. DA-334-2004:** Refundable security deposits are NOT considered income and are NOT subject to withholding at the time of receipt. The deposit is recorded as a **liability** on the lessor's books — it is trust money, not earned income.

**RMC No. 11-2024 (January 22, 2024):** Confirms the deposit is recorded as a liability by the lessor (and as an asset by the lessee). Neither party recognizes income or deduction at the time of the deposit.

**Accounting entry at receipt:**
```
DR  Cash / Bank                    [deposit amount]
    CR  Security Deposit Liability     [deposit amount]
```

### 6b. At Application or Forfeiture — Becomes Taxable Income

This is the **triggering event** for taxation:

- **BIR Ruling No. 118-12:** Security deposits are taxable only upon forfeiture or application. When applied to cover unpaid rent or damages, the applied amount becomes **taxable income** in the year of application.
- **BIR Ruling No. 025-02:** Forfeited rental deposits are part of **gross receipts** of the lessor.
- **BIR Ruling No. DA-489-03:** Forfeited deposits constitute taxable income to the lessor.
- **BIR Ruling No. 033-00:** Forfeited rental deposits are income to the lessor and potentially deductible losses to the lessee.
- **RMC No. 11-2024:** Confirms the lessee is entitled to a deduction in the year conditions making it the lessor's property occur.

**Accounting entry at application:**
```
DR  Security Deposit Liability     [applied amount]
    CR  Rental Income                  [applied amount]
```

### 6c. VAT Implications on Forfeiture/Application

- **Revenue Regulation No. 16-2005:** Amounts forfeited or applied to lease-related obligations are part of **gross receipts for VAT purposes**.
- **BIR Ruling No. DA-593-04:** Confirmed that forfeited deposits in lease arrangements are VATable.
- Subject to **12% VAT** when the lessor is VAT-registered (gross receipts > PHP 3M/year).
- Residential leases at or below PHP 15,000/month are VAT-exempt.

### 6d. Expanded Withholding Tax (EWT) on Application

- **RR No. 2-98 (as amended by RR 11-2018 and RR 14-2021), Section 2.57.2:** 5% EWT applies to rental income.
- Once a deposit is applied or forfeited and reclassified as rent income, **5% EWT applies**.
- The paying party (lessee, if corporate) must issue **BIR Form 2307** and file via **BIR Form 1601-EQ**.
- If lessee has already left (commercial abandonment case), the withholding obligation shifts to the lessor's self-assessment.

### 6e. Advance Rent vs. Security Deposit Distinction

**Advance rent** is taxable income to the lessor **upon receipt** regardless of when the rental period it covers begins.

A true security deposit is not taxable until applied or forfeited. However, calling an advance payment a "deposit" does not change its character if it is earmarked as the last month's rent — the BIR looks to **substance over form**.

**Key distinction:** Does the lessor have an obligation to refund? If yes → deposit (not taxable at receipt). If no → advance rent (taxable at receipt).

## 7. Corporate Landlord (SEC-Registered) Specific Rules

No separate "corporate landlord" statute for domestic SEC-registered entities holding tenant security deposits.

**Income recognition:** Upon forfeiture or application, the income is reported under "Other Income" or "Rental Income" in:
- **BIR Form 1702Q** (quarterly income tax returns)
- **BIR Form 1702** (annual corporate income tax return)

**Tax rates:**
- RCIT: **25%** for most corporations
- **20% RCIT** for domestic corporations with net taxable income ≤ PHP 5M AND total assets ≤ PHP 100M (CREATE Law, RA 11534, 2021)
- MCIT: 2% of gross income (if higher than RCIT)

**VAT returns:** Applied/forfeited deposits must be reported in:
- **BIR Form 2550M** (monthly VAT declaration) — if applicable
- **BIR Form 2550Q** (quarterly VAT return)

**SEC/PFRS financial reporting:** SEC-registered corporations must:
- Reclassify forfeited deposits from liabilities to income under PFRS 15 (Revenue from Contracts with Customers)
- Disclose in notes to audited financial statements filed with the SEC

**Note:** SEC Memorandum Circular No. 2-2012 (security deposit with SEC for foreign branch offices) is a completely separate concept from tenant security deposits and does NOT apply to domestic corporations.

## 9. Practical Implications for a Las Piñas SEC-Registered Rental Corporation

### Residential Units (Rent-Controlled)
- Collect max 2 months deposit + 1 month advance
- Must bank the deposit in a dedicated account
- Must track accrued bank interest per tenant
- At lease end: return deposit + all accrued interest within 1 month of turnover
- If applying deposit to unpaid rent: reclassify to income; include in gross receipts for VAT and income tax

### Commercial Units
- No deposit cap; negotiate freely
- Trust money doctrine still applies — don't co-mingle with operating funds
- Return within ~30 days of turnover (document condition with photos/inspection report)
- Interest is contractual unless landlord delays after demand (then 6% legal rate)
- Same tax reclassification rules apply upon application or forfeiture

### Key Tracking Requirements
1. Per-tenant deposit ledger: amount collected, date, bank account reference
2. Interest accrual tracking (residential units)
3. Deduction log with official receipt references (for itemized deductions)
4. Reclassification journal entries when applied/forfeited
5. VAT and EWT computation on applied amounts
