## CLAIM-BY-CLAIM VERIFICATION

### Claim 1: RA 9646 (RESA) does NOT prescribe commission rates — rates are contractual per Civil Code Art. 1305

**Verdict: CONFIRMED (with minor correction)**

All sources unanimously confirm that RA 9646 does not prescribe, mandate, or even recommend specific commission rates. Commissions are contractual, governed by freedom of contract principles.

**Correction:** The correct Civil Code article for freedom/autonomy of contracts is **Art. 1306** (not 1305). Art. 1305 defines what a contract is ("a meeting of minds between two persons..."). Art. 1306 states: "The contracting parties may establish such stipulations, clauses, terms and conditions as they may deem convenient, provided they are not contrary to law, morals, good customs, public order, or public policy." The primary source's reference to Art. 1305 is not wrong (it does establish the contractual basis), but Art. 1306 is the more precisely relevant provision for the claim that rates are "contractual."

**Sources confirming:** [1], [2], [4], [5], [6], [7], [19]

### Claim 2: Industry standard sale commission: 3-5% of selling price (5% most commonly cited)

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

Every practitioner source confirms 3-5% as the standard range, with 5% being the most commonly cited figure. Multiple sources use the phrase "industry standard is 5%." Some sources cite 2.5-5% as the broader range for high-value properties, and up to 6% for medium-priced properties.

**Sources confirming:** [5], [6], [7], [8], [15], [22] — all independently state 3-5% with 5% as standard.

### Claim 3: Horizontal/economic housing commission: 6-7% (higher than standard)

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

Multiple sources confirm that horizontal projects, particularly economic/affordable housing, command higher commissions of 6-7%. The explanation offered is the competitive, high-demand nature of these properties and the effort required for lower-priced units.

**Sources confirming:** [5], [6], [8]

### Claim 4: Residential lease commission: 1 month's rent per year of lease term

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

This is consistently cited across practitioner sources. The formula is confirmed as: commission = monthly_rent x lease_years. For a 1-year lease, commission = 1 month's rent. Multiple sources also confirm the "one month per year of lease period" phrasing.

**Sources confirming:** [4], [18], [2], [5], forum practitioner posts

### Claim 5: Commercial lease commission: 3-6% of total lease value

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

Respicio & Co. (law firm) and multiple practitioner sources confirm 3-6% of total lease value (cumulative rent over entire term). The computation example in the primary source (₱100K/month x 60 months = ₱6M x 5% = ₱300K) is independently replicated by Respicio & Co. with the identical numbers.

**Sources confirming:** [4], [5], [6], [18]

### Claim 6: Common broker-salesperson splits: 50/50, 60/40, 80/20

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

All three split ratios are confirmed across multiple independent sources. The consensus is:
- **50/50** — most common starting point for new salespersons
- **60/40** — negotiated by experienced salespersons (salesperson gets 60%)
- **80/20** — for top producers (agent gets 80%) OR new agents (firm gets 80%, depending on the arrangement)
- **100% to agent** — also mentioned as possible with desk/platform fees

**Note on ambiguity:** The primary source lists "80/20 — firm retains 80%, agent 20% (common for new agents)" but some secondary sources describe 80/20 the other way (agent 80%, firm 20%) for top producers. Both arrangements exist; the direction of the split depends on the specific brokerage model.

**Sources confirming:** [5], [6], [8], [22]

### Claim 8: EWT rates per RR 10-2013: 10% (<=₱720K gross) or 15% (>₱720K) for PRC-licensed practitioners

**Verdict: CORRECTED — Rates are outdated; RR 10-2013 has been superseded by RR 11-2018 (TRAIN Law)**

The RR 10-2013 rates cited in the primary source were accurate **at the time they were enacted (2013)**:
- Licensed RESPs: 10% (gross <=₱720K) or 15% (gross >₱720K)
- Unlicensed: flat 10%

However, these rates were **superseded** by RR No. 11-2018 (implementing the TRAIN Law, RA 10963), effective January 1, 2018. The current EWT rates for **individual** professionals (including licensed real estate brokers) are:

| Practitioner Status | Annual Gross Income | Current EWT Rate (post-TRAIN) |
|---|---|---|
| Individual PRC-licensed RESP | <= ₱3,000,000 | **5%** |
| Individual PRC-licensed RESP | > ₱3,000,000 | **10%** |
| Corporate payee | <= ₱720,000 | 10% |
| Corporate payee | > ₱720,000 | 15% |

**Critical finding:** The primary source presents the pre-TRAIN rates (RR 10-2013) as if they are current. For individual licensed brokers, the correct current rates are **5%/10%** (with a ₱3M threshold), not 10%/15% (with a ₱720K threshold). The ₱720K threshold and 10%/15% rates now apply only to corporate payees.

**Requirement for lower rate:** A sworn declaration of gross receipts (not an "affidavit" per RR 30-2003) must be submitted to withholding agents by January 15 each year. Without it, the higher rate (10% for individuals, 15% for corporations) applies by default.

**Sources confirming correction:** [11], [12], [13], [14], [16], [20]

### Claim 9: Affidavit requirement per RR 30-2003 for 10% rate

**Verdict: CORRECTED — Partially accurate but outdated**

RR 30-2003 did establish the affidavit/sworn declaration requirement, and this concept carries forward. However, under the current regime (RR 11-2018), the requirement has been updated:
- The document is now called a "Sworn Declaration of Gross Receipts/Sales" (Annexes B-1 to B-3 of RR 11-2018)
- It must be submitted by **January 15** of each year (or prior to initial payment)
- It qualifies individual professionals for the **5% rate** (not 10% as stated in the primary source)
- Failure to submit results in the **10% rate** being applied (not 15%)

The primary source correctly identifies that a sworn declaration is required for the lower rate, but the specific rates and thresholds are outdated.

**Sources confirming:** [12], [14], [20]

### Claim 10: VAT threshold: 12% if broker gross > ₱3,000,000

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

The TRAIN Law (RA 10963) raised the VAT threshold from ~₱1,919,500 to ₱3,000,000. This threshold remains in effect as of 2025-2026. Brokers with annual gross receipts exceeding ₱3M must register as VAT taxpayers and charge 12% VAT on their services. Below the threshold, brokers pay 3% percentage tax instead (or may elect the 8% flat income tax option).

**Sources confirming:** [20], [21], Quaderno Philippines VAT Guide, Taxumo TRAIN Law Primer

### Claim 11: Salesperson can only receive compensation from supervising broker (RESA Sec. 31)

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

The exact statutory language is confirmed: "No salesperson shall be entitled to receive or demand a fee, commission or compensation of any kind from any person, other than the duly licensed real estate broker who has direct control and supervision over him, for any service rendered or work done by such salesperson in any real estate transaction."

PRBRES Resolution No. 11 (Series of 2021) further implements supervision guidelines under Sec. 31.

**Sources confirming:** [9], [19], PRC Resolution No. 11 (2021), multiple law firm commentaries

### Claim 12: Multi-tier brokerage distribution: 20% firm retention, then 60/15/10 splits (iRealtee model)

**Verdict: CONFIRMED (as one model, not universal)**

iRealtee's own website and Manila Times coverage confirm this specific distribution model: brokerage retains 20%, remaining 80% is distributed as 60% to selling agent, 15% override to unit manager, 10% to division manager. The primary source correctly notes that percentages sum to 85% of the remaining amount (60+15+10), leaving 15% unallocated (described in iRealtee materials as going to "other overrides or reserves").

The primary source correctly identified this as the "iRealtee model" and did not claim it as a universal standard.

**Sources confirming:** [15], Manila Times (Jan 2026 coverage of iRealtee launch)

### Claim 13: Listing broker/selling broker MLS split: 50/50 standard

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

The PHMLS (CEREBMLS) participation rules explicitly establish a 50/50 professional fee split between listing broker and selling broker. PhilHome also references a 50/50 split (2.5% each on a 5% total commission) for co-brokered transactions. This is the standard in Philippine MLS operations.

**Sources confirming:** [17] (CEREBMLS rules), PhilHome FAQs, Rephil MLS documentation

---

### Claim 14: Procuring cause doctrine governs commission entitlement in PH

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

Philippine jurisprudence firmly applies the "efficient procuring cause" doctrine (rooted in Civil Code Art. 1875). Multiple Supreme Court decisions were found confirming this:
- *Inland Realty Corp. v. CA* (G.R. No. 112051, 1996) — broker earns commission upon producing ready, willing, able buyer
- *Prudential Bank v. CA* (G.R. No. 103957, 1993) — original broker entitled over subsequent ones
- *Ticong v. Malim* (G.R. No. 220785) — actions must be foundation of negotiation, not just introduction

**Sources confirming:** [1], [2], [10], SC E-Library decisions

---

### Claim 15: Renewal commission: broker entitled to commission again on lease renewal

**Verdict: CONFIRMED (as industry practice, not statutory right)**

Multiple practitioner sources confirm that brokers are customarily entitled to commission on lease renewal. A PRC-licensed broker quoted on PhilPropertyExpert explicitly states: "If there is a renewal of contract, the broker is entitled to a commission again." However, this is a contractual/industry practice, not a statutory guarantee. Entitlement depends on the terms of the brokerage agreement.

One source notes that "some owners try to be cheap and skip the brokers" on renewals, indicating this is an area of frequent dispute.

**Sources confirming:** [18], [4], forum practitioner posts

### B. Correct VAT Threshold as of 2025-2026

**Finding:** The VAT threshold remains at **₱3,000,000** as established by the TRAIN Law (RA 10963, effective 2018). There are discussions about potential increases for MSMEs under future tax reform packages, but no legislation has raised the threshold above ₱3M as of February 2026.

The primary source correctly states ₱3,000,000.

Below the threshold, brokers may opt for either:
- 3% percentage tax on gross receipts, OR
- 8% flat income tax rate on gross sales/receipts (in lieu of graduated income tax AND percentage tax)

### E. Commission Structure for Foreclosed Property Sales

**Finding:** Foreclosed property (ROPA/ROPOA/REO) broker commissions vary by institution:

- **Pag-IBIG Fund:** Historically paid brokers up to **5%** (separate from bid price, does not affect minimum bid). However, since **September 2019**, Pag-IBIG removed its list of accredited brokers and now directs buyers to deal with the Fund directly. Buyers should be cautious of unauthorized agents.

- **BPI (Buena Mano):** Reported to offer **5%** commission to accredited brokers. Has a formal "Top Brass" awards program for top-performing brokers.

- **BDO:** Requires broker accreditation. Specific commission rate not publicly disclosed.

- **Metrobank:** Handled through Acquired Assets Management and Disposal Group (AAMDG). Specific rate not publicly disclosed.

- **Security Bank:** Offers "higher commission" to accredited brokers + 1% additional discount for cash/multi-property purchases.

**General range:** 3-5% of selling price, consistent with standard market commissions. Banks pay commissions separately (not added to the selling price).

**Key regulatory note:** BSP policy encourages banks to dispose of ROPA promptly. The 5-year holding period limit (with potential extension) creates urgency that may support higher broker commissions on difficult-to-sell assets.

**Sources:** ForeclosurePhilippines.com, BSP MORB Section 382, Pag-IBIG Fund website

### F. Commission for Property Management Services

**Finding:** Property management fees are distinct from brokerage commissions and follow a separate structure:

| Service Type | Standard Fee | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Residential property management | ~10% of monthly rent | Percentage of rental income |
| Commercial property management | 3-10% of rental income | Varies by building class, location |
| Fixed-fee model | Varies (e.g., ₱5,000-₱15,000/month) | Per-property fixed fee |
| Per-square-meter model | ₱50-100+/sqm | Based on leased area size |

Property management is a separate licensed real estate service under RESA (Sec. 3(g)). Property managers may or may not be the same person as the leasing broker. Management fees are recurring (monthly), while brokerage commissions are transactional (upon lease signing).

**Sources:** KondoKo, RichestPH (commercial property management), RE Forum PH

### G. Finder's Fee vs. Broker Commission — Legal Distinction in PH

**Finding:** There is a meaningful legal distinction:

| Aspect | Broker Commission | Finder's Fee |
|---|---|---|
| **Licensing** | Requires PRC license under RESA | No license required (limited role) |
| **Scope** | Full brokerage: negotiate, mediate, close | Limited to introduction/referral only |
| **Rate** | 3-5% of selling price (standard) | Typically 1-2% (lower, negotiated) |
| **Legal basis** | Contract of agency (Civil Code) | Quantum meruit / contractual |
| **Enforceability** | Enforceable if licensed + procuring cause | Enforceable if limited to referral scope |
| **Risk** | Regulated, penalties for unlicensed practice | Finder must NOT cross into brokerage activities |

**Critical legal risk:** If a "finder" performs activities that constitute real estate brokerage (negotiating, mediating, advertising), they may be deemed to be engaged in unlicensed practice under RESA, which carries penalties of up to ₱200,000 fine and/or 4 years imprisonment. The distinction is functional, not just nominal.

Finder's fees can be enforced under **quantum meruit** (Art. 2142, Civil Code) even without a written contract, but written agreements are strongly recommended.

**Sources:** [2], Genie AI (PH Finder's Fee Agreement template), [1]

### H. Commission on Rent-to-Own Transactions

**Finding:** No standard industry convention was found specifically for rent-to-own commission computation. The treatment appears to depend on how the transaction is structured:

1. **If treated as a sale from inception** (most common for developer rent-to-own programs): Commission is computed as **3-5% of the total contract price (TCP)**, same as a standard sale. The broker receives commission based on the TCP, not the monthly rent.

2. **If treated as a lease with option to purchase:** The lease phase may be compensated as a residential lease commission (1 month's rent per year), and if/when the purchase option is exercised, a separate sale commission may apply. This is less common and would need to be explicitly agreed upon.

3. **Developer rent-to-own programs (e.g., Pag-IBIG Rent-to-Own):** These are generally structured as installment sales. The commission is typically computed on the selling price, not on the rental payments.

**Key observation:** The primary source does not address rent-to-own transactions. This is a gap that should be filled, as rent-to-own is a significant transaction type in Philippine real estate, especially for socialized and economic housing.

**Sources:** General practitioner sources; no authoritative specific guidance found

## CONFLICTS BETWEEN SOURCES

### Conflict 1: EWT Rate — Primary Source vs. Current Law

The primary source presents RR 10-2013 rates (10%/15% at ₱720K threshold) as current. Multiple secondary sources confirm these were superseded by RR 11-2018 (TRAIN Law), which sets 5%/10% at ₱3M threshold for individual professionals. **The primary source is outdated on this point.**

However, one secondary source (Respicio & Co. — Taxation on RE Agent Commission) also cites 10%/15% at ₱3M, which appears to conflate the corporate rates with individual rates. This may indicate some practitioner confusion in the field. The authoritative sources (Tax and Accounting Center, PWC Philippines, Forvis Mazars) clearly distinguish individual vs. corporate rates.

**Correct current rates for individual licensed RE brokers:**
- 5% EWT if gross income <= ₱3M
- 10% EWT if gross income > ₱3M

### Conflict 2: 80/20 Split Direction

Some sources describe 80/20 as "firm 80%, agent 20%" (for new agents), while others describe it as "agent 80%, firm 20%" (for top producers). Both arrangements exist in practice. The primary source only describes the first interpretation.

### Conflict 3: SMDC 4.75%

The primary source states this is "publicly stated." No public source was found confirming this exact figure. It may be accurate but appears to be shared in accreditation materials, not in public-facing documents.

## ADDITIONAL COMPUTATIONS / EDGE CASES DISCOVERED

1. **8% Flat Tax Option:** Brokers with annual gross below ₱3M can elect an 8% flat income tax rate (in lieu of graduated rates + percentage tax). This simplifies computation but is not mentioned in the primary source.

2. **CWT Computation on Net Fee (Exclusive of VAT):** For VAT-registered brokers, EWT is computed on the professional fee net of VAT. The VAT portion is not subject to withholding. This is a computation subtlety not addressed in the primary source.

3. **Pre-owned Property Commission:** Some sources cite 2.5% as the floor for high-value pre-owned properties, lower than the 3% cited as the minimum for developer sales.

4. **Referral Fee Deduction from Agent Share:** When a referral is involved, the referring agent's fee (commonly 20% of the salesperson's share) is deducted from the salesperson's portion, not from the gross commission. This adds another computation layer.

5. **BSP 5-Year ROPA Holding Limit:** Banks must dispose of foreclosed properties within 5 years (extendable to 10 for justified cases), which may affect commission negotiations.

6. **Prescription Periods for Commission Claims:** 10 years for written contracts (Art. 1144), 6 years for oral contracts (Art. 1145). Relevant to dispute computation.
